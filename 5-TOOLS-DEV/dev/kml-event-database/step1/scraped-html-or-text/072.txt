<p><b>Bubastis</b> (<a href="/wiki/Copts" title="Copts">Bohairic Coptic</a>: <span title="Coptic-language text"><span lang="cop">Ⲡⲟⲩⲃⲁⲥϯ</span></span> <i>Poubasti</i>; <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a>: <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">Βούβαστις</span></span> <i>Boubastis</i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">Βούβαστος</span></span> <i>Boubastos</i><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>), also known in <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> as <b>Tell-Basta</b> or in <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">Egyptian</a> as <b>Per-Bast</b>, was an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egyptian</a> city. Bubastis is often identified with the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">biblical</a> <i>Pi-Beseth</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span lang="he" dir="rtl">פי-בסת</span> <i>py-bst</i>, <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ezekiel" title="Book of Ezekiel">Ezekiel</a> 30:17).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was the capital of its own <a href="/wiki/Nome_(Egypt)" title="Nome (Egypt)">nome</a>, located along the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">River Nile</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Nile_Delta" title="Nile Delta">Delta</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Egypt" title="Lower Egypt">Lower Egypt</a>, and notable as a <a href="/wiki/Holy_city" title="Holy city">center of worship</a> for the feline goddess <a href="/wiki/Bastet" title="Bastet">Bastet</a>, and therefore the principal depository in Egypt of <a href="/wiki/Animal_mummy#Cats" title="Animal mummy">mummies of cats</a>. 
</p><p>Its <a href="/wiki/Ruins" title="Ruins">ruins</a> are located in the suburbs of the modern city of <a href="/wiki/Zagazig" title="Zagazig">Zagazig</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Etymology">Etymology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bubastis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The name of Bubastis in <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">Egyptian</a> is <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pr-b%EA%9C%A3stt" class="extiw" title="wikt:pr-bꜣstt"><i>Pr-Bȝst.t</i></a>, conventionally pronounced <i>Per-Bast</i> but its Earlier Egyptian pronunciation can be reconstructed as /ˈpaɾu-buˈʀistit/. It is a compound of Egyptian <span title="Ancient Egyptian-language text"><i lang="egy"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pr" class="extiw" title="wikt:pr">pr</a></i></span> (“house") and the name of the goddess <a href="/wiki/Bastet" title="Bastet">Bastet</a>; thus the phrase means "House of Bast".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In later forms of Egyptian, sound shifts had altered the pronunciation. In Bohairic Coptic, the name is rendered <span title="Coptic-language text"><span lang="cop">Ⲡⲟⲩⲃⲁⲥϯ</span></span>, <span title="Coptic-language text"><span lang="cop">Ⲡⲟⲩⲁⲥϯ</span></span> or <span title="Coptic-language text"><span lang="cop">Ⲃⲟⲩⲁⲥϯ</span></span>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bubastis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Hathor_capital_-_Pharaoh_exhibit_-_Cleveland_Museum_of_Art_(27360310044).jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Hathor_capital_-_Pharaoh_exhibit_-_Cleveland_Museum_of_Art_%2827360310044%29.jpg/220px-Hathor_capital_-_Pharaoh_exhibit_-_Cleveland_Museum_of_Art_%2827360310044%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Hathor_capital_-_Pharaoh_exhibit_-_Cleveland_Museum_of_Art_%2827360310044%29.jpg/330px-Hathor_capital_-_Pharaoh_exhibit_-_Cleveland_Museum_of_Art_%2827360310044%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Hathor_capital_-_Pharaoh_exhibit_-_Cleveland_Museum_of_Art_%2827360310044%29.jpg/440px-Hathor_capital_-_Pharaoh_exhibit_-_Cleveland_Museum_of_Art_%2827360310044%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1667" data-file-height="2500" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Hathor" title="Hathor">Hathor</a> capital from the Temple of Bubastis in the collection of the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<p>Bubastis served as the capital of the <i><a href="/wiki/Nome_(Egypt)" title="Nome (Egypt)">nome</a></i> of <i>Am-Khent</i>, the 18th nome of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Egypt" title="Lower Egypt">Lower Egypt</a>. Bubastis was situated southwest of <a href="/wiki/Tanis" title="Tanis">Tanis</a>, upon the eastern side of the <a href="/wiki/Pelusiac_branch" class="mw-redirect" title="Pelusiac branch">Pelusiac branch</a> of the Nile. The <i>nome</i> and city of Bubastis were allotted to the Calasirian division of the Egyptian war-caste.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Second_Dynasty">Second Dynasty</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bubastis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Second Dynasty"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In a later account by the Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Manetho" title="Manetho">Manetho</a>, it's stated that a "chasm opened near Bubastis and many perished" during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Hotepsekhemwy" title="Hotepsekhemwy">Hotepsekhemwy</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Second Dynasty of Egypt">Second Dynasty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WGW_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WGW-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Twelfth_Dynasty">Twelfth Dynasty</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bubastis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Twelfth Dynasty"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In the Middle Kingdom, Tell Basta was the site of a large mudbrick palace (16,000 sqm) dated to the Twelfth Dynasty.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It has been thought to be a residence of <a href="/wiki/Amenemhat_III" title="Amenemhat III">Amenemhat III</a>. A limestone lintel shows the king during his <a href="/wiki/Sed_festival" title="Sed festival">Heb Sed Festival</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Second_Intermediate_Period">Second Intermediate Period</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bubastis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Second Intermediate Period"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Following the Twelfth Dynasty, the Middle Kingdom faded out. At Bubastis, a red granite architrave belonged to <a href="/wiki/Sekhemrekhutawy_Khabaw" title="Sekhemrekhutawy Khabaw">Sekhemre Khutawy Khabaw</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In another monument found at Tanis, he is mentioned along with <a href="/wiki/Hor" title="Hor">Hor</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Twenty-Second_Dynasty">Twenty-Second Dynasty</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bubastis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Twenty-Second Dynasty"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>It became a royal residence after <a href="/wiki/Shoshenq_I" title="Shoshenq I">Shoshenq I</a>, the first ruler and founder of the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-second_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twenty-second Dynasty of Egypt">22nd Dynasty</a>, became <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">pharaoh</a> in 943 BC. Bubastis was its height during this dynasty and the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-third_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twenty-third Dynasty of Egypt">23rd</a>. It declined after the conquest by <a href="/wiki/Cambyses_II" title="Cambyses II">Cambyses II</a> in 525 BC, which heralded the end of the Saite <a href="/wiki/Twenty-sixth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Twenty-sixth Dynasty of Egypt">26th Dynasty</a> and the start of the <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Achaemenid Empire</a>.
</p><p>The Twenty Second Dynasty of Egyptian monarchs consisted of nine, or, according to <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> of three Bubastite kings, and during their reigns the city was one of the most considerable places in the Delta. Immediately to the south of Bubastis were the allotments of land with which <a href="/wiki/Psamtik_I" title="Psamtik I">Psamtik I</a> rewarded the services of his <a href="/wiki/Ionians" title="Ionians">Ionian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carian" class="mw-redirect" title="Carian">Carian</a> mercenaries;<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and on the northern side of the city commenced the <a href="/wiki/Canal_of_the_Pharaohs" title="Canal of the Pharaohs">Canal of the Pharaohs</a>, which Pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Necho_II" title="Necho II">Necho II</a> began (but never finished) to go between the Nile and the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Persian_rule_and_decline">Persian rule and decline</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bubastis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Persian rule and decline"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>After Bubastis was taken by the Persians, its walls were dismantled.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> From this period it gradually declined, although it appears in ecclesiastical annals among the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_see" title="Episcopal see">episcopal sees</a> of the province <a href="/wiki/Augustamnica" title="Augustamnica">Augustamnica Secunda</a>. Bubastite coins of the age of <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a> exist.
The following is the description which <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> gives of Bubastis, as it appeared shortly after the period of the Persian invasion, 525 BC, and Hamilton remarks that the plan of the ruins remarkably warrants the accuracy of this historical eye-witness:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Temples there are more spacious and costlier than that of Bubastis, but none so pleasant to behold. It is after the following fashion. Except at the entrance, it is surrounded by water: for two canals branch off from the river, and run as far as the entrance to the temple: yet neither canal mingles with the other, but one runs on this side, and the other on that. Each canal is a hundred feet wide, and its banks are lined with trees. The propylaea are sixty feet in height, and are adorned with sculptures (probably intaglios in relief) nine feet high, and of excellent workmanship. The Temple being in the middle of the city is looked down upon from all sides as you walk around; and this comes from the city having been raised, whereas the temple itself has not been moved, but remains in its original place. Quite round the temple there goes a wall, adorned with sculptures. Within the inclosure is a grove of fair tall trees, planted around a large building in which is the effigy (of Bast). The form of that temple is square, each side being a stadium in length. In a line with the entrance is a road built of stone about three stadia long, leading eastwards through the public market. The road is about 400 feet (120&#160;m) broad, and is flanked by exceeding tall trees. It leads to the temple of Hermes.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Religion">Religion</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bubastis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Religion"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:AmenophisIIRelief-BritishMuseum-August19-08.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/AmenophisIIRelief-BritishMuseum-August19-08.jpg/220px-AmenophisIIRelief-BritishMuseum-August19-08.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="104" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/AmenophisIIRelief-BritishMuseum-August19-08.jpg/330px-AmenophisIIRelief-BritishMuseum-August19-08.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/AmenophisIIRelief-BritishMuseum-August19-08.jpg/440px-AmenophisIIRelief-BritishMuseum-August19-08.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3504" data-file-height="1662" /></a><figcaption>Relief of the pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Amenhotep_II" title="Amenhotep II">Amenhotep II</a>, made of red granite. It depicts the pharaoh worshiping the god <a href="/wiki/Amun" title="Amun">Amun</a>. From the <a href="/wiki/Eighteenth_Dynasty_of_Egypt" title="Eighteenth Dynasty of Egypt">18th Dynasty</a>, circa 1430 BC, with an additional inscription by <a href="/wiki/Seti_I" title="Seti I">Seti I</a> (circa 1290 BC). Originally from Bubastis, <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<p>Bubastis was a center of worship for the feline goddess <a href="/wiki/Bastet" title="Bastet">Bastet</a>, sometimes called <i>Bubastis</i> after the city, who the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greeks</a> identified with <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cats_in_ancient_Egypt" title="Cats in ancient Egypt">cat was the sacred and peculiar animal</a> of Bast, who is represented with the head of a cat or a <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lioness</a> and frequently accompanies the deity <a href="/wiki/Ptah" title="Ptah">Ptah</a> in monumental inscriptions. The tombs at Bubastis were accordingly the principal depository in Egypt of the <a href="/wiki/Animal_mummy#Cats" title="Animal mummy">mummies of the cat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The most distinguished features of the city and nome of Bubastis were its oracle of Bast, the splendid temple of that goddess and the annual procession in honor of her. The oracle gained in popularity and importance after the influx of Greek settlers into the Delta, since the identification of Bast with Artemis attracted to her shrine both native Egyptians and foreigners.
</p><p>The festival of Bubastis was considered the most joyous and gorgeous of all in the Egyptian calendar as described by <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1244412712"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Barges and river craft of every description, filled with men and women, floated leisurely down the Nile. The men played on pipes of lotus. the women on cymbals and tambourines, and such as had no instruments accompanied the music with clapping of hands and dances, and other joyous gestures. Thus did they while on the river: but when they came to a town on its banks, the barges were made fast, and the pilgrims disembarked, and the women sang, playfully mocked the women of that town and threw their clothes over their head. When they reached Bubastis, then held they a wondrously solemn feast: and more wine of the grape was drank in those days than in all the rest of the year. Such was the manner of this festival: and, it is said, that as many as seven hundred thousand pilgrims have been known to celebrate the Feast of Bast at the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>

<p>Extant documents mention the names of three Christian bishops of Bubastis of the 4th and 5th centuries:
</p>
<ul><li>Harpocration, one of the bishops ordained by <a href="/wiki/Melitius_of_Lycopolis" title="Melitius of Lycopolis">Melitius of Lycopolis</a> listed in 325</li>
<li>Hermon, a contemporary of <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_of_Alexandria" title="Athanasius of Alexandria">Athanasius of Alexandria</a>, in about 362</li>
<li>Iulianus at the <a href="/wiki/Second_Council_of_Ephesus" title="Second Council of Ephesus">Second Council of Ephesus</a> in 449<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Excavations">Excavations</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bubastis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Excavations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The tomb of the late <a href="/wiki/New_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="New Kingdom of Egypt">New Kingdom</a> <a href="/wiki/Vizier_(Ancient_Egypt)" title="Vizier (Ancient Egypt)">vizier</a> <a href="/wiki/Iuty" title="Iuty">Iuty</a> was discovered in December 1964 in the "Cemetery of the Nobles" of Bubastis by the Egyptian archaeologist Shafik Farid.
</p><p>Since 2008, the German-Egyptian "Tell Basta Project" has been conducting excavations at Bubastis. Previously, in March 2004, a well preserved copy of the <a href="/wiki/Decree_of_Canopus" title="Decree of Canopus">Decree of Canopus</a> was discovered in the city.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
