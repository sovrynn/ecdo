<p>The <b>Laurentide ice sheet</b> (LIS) was a massive <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">sheet of ice</a> that covered millions of square miles, including most of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and a large portion of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_United_States" title="Northern United States">Northern United States</a>, multiple times during the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciation</a> epochs, from 2.58 million years ago to the present.<sup id="cite_ref-ICS_StratChart_2022-02_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ICS_StratChart_2022-02-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The last advance covered most of northern North America between c. 95,000 and c. 20,000 years before the present day and, among other geomorphological effects, gouged out the five <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> and the hosts of smaller lakes of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Shield" title="Canadian Shield">Canadian Shield</a>. These lakes extend from the eastern <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a>, through most of northern Canada, and the upper <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwestern United States</a> (<a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>) to the <a href="/wiki/Finger_Lakes" title="Finger Lakes">Finger Lakes</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Lake_Champlain" title="Lake Champlain">Lake Champlain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_George_(lake),_New_York" title="Lake George (lake), New York">Lake George</a> areas of <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, across the northern <a href="/wiki/Appalachians" class="mw-redirect" title="Appalachians">Appalachians</a> into and through all of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>.
</p><p>At times, the ice sheet's southern margin included the present-day sites of coastal towns of the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">Northeastern United States</a>, and cities such as <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and Great Lakes coastal cities and towns as far south as <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>, and then followed the present course of the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri River</a> up to the northern slopes of the <a href="/wiki/Cypress_Hills_(Canada)" title="Cypress Hills (Canada)">Cypress Hills</a>, beyond which it merged with the <a href="/wiki/Cordilleran_Ice_Sheet" class="mw-redirect" title="Cordilleran Ice Sheet">Cordilleran Ice Sheet</a>. The ice coverage extended approximately as far south as 38 degrees latitude mid-continent.<sup id="cite_ref-dyke_prest_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dyke_prest-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Description">Description</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laurentide_ice_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>This ice sheet was the primary feature of the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> epoch in North America, commonly referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">ice age</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Illinoian" title="Pre-Illinoian">Pre-Illinoian Stage</a>, the Laurentide Ice Sheet extended as far south as the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a> valleys. It was up to 2&#160;mi (3.2&#160;km) thick in <a href="/wiki/Nunavik" title="Nunavik">Nunavik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, but much thinner at its edges, where <a href="/wiki/Nunatak" title="Nunatak">nunataks</a> were common in hilly areas. It created much of the surface geology of southern Canada and the northern United States, leaving behind glacially scoured valleys, <a href="/wiki/Moraines" class="mw-redirect" title="Moraines">moraines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eskers" class="mw-redirect" title="Eskers">eskers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glacial_till" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial till">glacial till</a>. It also caused many changes to the shape, size, and drainage of the Great Lakes. As but one of many examples, near the end of the last ice age, <a href="/wiki/Glacial_Lake_Iroquois" title="Glacial Lake Iroquois">Lake Iroquois</a> extended well beyond the boundaries of present-day <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">Lake Ontario</a>, and drained down the Hudson River into the Atlantic Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Its cycles of growth and melting were a decisive influence on global <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> during its existence. This is because it served to divert the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> southward, which would otherwise flow from the relatively warm <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> through <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>. That gave the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">Southwestern United States</a>, otherwise a desert, abundant rainfall during ice ages, in extreme contrast to most other parts of the world which became exceedingly dry, though the effect of ice sheets in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> had an analogous effect on the rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, parts of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, possibly western <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> in winter, as well as <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.
</p>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg/200px-Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="273" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg/300px-Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg/400px-Sea_Ice_off_Baffin_Island.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4400" data-file-height="6000" /></a><figcaption>The <a href="/wiki/Barnes_Ice_Cap" title="Barnes Ice Cap">Barnes Ice Cap</a>, containing remnants of the Laurentide Ice Sheet.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Its melting also caused major disruptions to the global climate cycle, because the huge influx of low-<a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> water into the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_River" title="Mackenzie River">Mackenzie River</a><sup id="cite_ref-Murton_Bateman_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Murton_Bateman-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> is believed to have disrupted the formation of <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a>, the very saline, cold, deep water that flows from the <a href="/wiki/Greenland_Sea" title="Greenland Sea">Greenland Sea</a>. That interrupted the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>, creating the brief <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> cold epoch and a temporary re-advance of the ice sheet,<sup id="cite_ref-Broeckery_Denton_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Broeckery_Denton-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> which did not retreat from <a href="/wiki/Nunavik" title="Nunavik">Nunavik</a> until 6,500 years ago.
</p><p>After the end of the Younger Dryas, the Laurentide Ice Sheet retreated rapidly to the north, becoming limited to only the Canadian Shield until even it became deglaciated.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The ultimate collapse of the Laurentide Ice Sheet is also suspected to have influenced European agriculture indirectly through the rise of global sea levels.
</p><p>Canada's oldest ice is in remnants of the LIS: the <a href="/wiki/Barnes_Ice_Cap" title="Barnes Ice Cap">Barnes Ice Cap</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Penny_Ice_Cap" title="Penny Ice Cap">Penny Ice Cap</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Ice_centers">Ice centers</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laurentide_ice_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ice centers"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a>, the Laurentide ice sheet reached from the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> eastward through the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>, into <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, covering nearly all of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> east of the Rocky Mountains.<sup id="cite_ref-Hill2006_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hill2006-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
Three major ice centers formed in North America: the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_ice_sheet" class="mw-redirect" title="Labrador ice sheet">Labrador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keewatin_ice_sheet" class="mw-redirect" title="Keewatin ice sheet">Keewatin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cordilleran_Ice_Sheet" class="mw-redirect" title="Cordilleran Ice Sheet">Cordilleran</a>. The Cordilleran covered the region from the Pacific Ocean to the eastern front of the Rocky Mountains and the Labrador and Keewatin fields are referred to as the Laurentide Ice Sheet. Central North America has evidence of the numerous lobes and sublobes. The Keewatin covered the western interior plains of North America from the <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_River" title="Mackenzie River">Mackenzie River</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri River</a> and the upper reaches of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>. The Labrador covered spread over <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Canada" title="Eastern Canada">eastern Canada</a> and the northeastern part of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> abutting the Keewatin lobe in the western <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River#Upper_Mississippi" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi valley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hill2006_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hill2006-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Keewatin_ice_dome">Keewatin ice dome</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laurentide_ice_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Keewatin ice dome"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The Keewatin ice dome has had four or five primary lobes identified ice divides extending from a dome over west-central <a href="/wiki/Kivalliq_Region" title="Kivalliq Region">Keewatin</a> (Kivalliq). Two of the lobes abut the adjacent Labrador and Foxe-Baffin ice domes. The primary lobes flow (1) towards <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>; (2) toward <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a>; (3) towards the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Boothia" title="Gulf of Boothia">Gulf of Boothia</a>, and (4) towards the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_Sea" title="Beaufort Sea">Beaufort Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dyke1987_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke1987-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Labrador_ice_dome">Labrador ice dome</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laurentide_ice_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Labrador ice dome"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The Labrador dome flowed across all of <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_St._Lawrence" title="Gulf of St. Lawrence">Gulf of St. Lawrence</a>, completely covering the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Provinces" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritime Provinces">Maritime Provinces</a>. The Appalachian Ice Complex, flowed from the <a href="/wiki/Gasp%C3%A9_Peninsula" title="Gaspé Peninsula">Gaspé Peninsula</a> over <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Magdalen_Islands" title="Magdalen Islands">Magdalen Shelf</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dyke1987_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke1987-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
The Labrador flow extended across the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/St._Lawrence_River" title="St. Lawrence River">St. Lawrence River</a>, reaching the <a href="/wiki/Gasp%C3%A9_Peninsula" title="Gaspé Peninsula">Gaspé Peninsula</a> and across <a href="/wiki/Chaleur_Bay" title="Chaleur Bay">Chaleur Bay</a>. From the Escuminac center on the <a href="/wiki/Magdalen_Islands" title="Magdalen Islands">Magdalen Shelf</a>, flowed onto the <a href="/wiki/Acadian_Peninsula" title="Acadian Peninsula">Acadian Peninsula</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a> and southeastward, onto the Gaspe, burying the western end of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a> and reached the head of <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Fundy" title="Bay of Fundy">Bay of Fundy</a>. From the Gaspereau center, on the divide crossing New Brunswick flowed into the Bay of Fundy and Chaleur Bay.<sup id="cite_ref-Dyke1987_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke1987-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In New York, the ice that covered Manhattan was about 2,000 feet high before it began to melt in about 16,000 BC. The ice in the area disappeared around 10,000 BC. The ground in the New York area has since risen by more than 150&#160;ft because of the removal of the <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">enormous weight of the melted ice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Foxe-Baffin_ice_dome">Foxe-Baffin ice dome</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laurentide_ice_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Foxe-Baffin ice dome"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The Foxe-Baffin ice dome was circular and centered over the <a href="/wiki/Foxe_Basin" title="Foxe Basin">Foxe Basin</a>. A major divide across the basin, created a westward flow across the <a href="/wiki/Melville_Peninsula" title="Melville Peninsula">Melville Peninsula</a>, from an eastward flow over <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southampton_Island" title="Southampton Island">Southampton Island</a>. Across southern Baffin Island, two divides created four additional lobes. The Penny Ice Divide split the <a href="/wiki/Cumberland_Peninsula" title="Cumberland Peninsula">Cumberland Peninsula</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Pangnirtung" title="Pangnirtung">Pangnirtung</a> created flow toward Home Bay on the north and Cumberland Sound on the south. The Amadjuak Ice Divide on the <a href="/wiki/Hall_Peninsula" title="Hall Peninsula">Hall Peninsula</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Iqaluit" title="Iqaluit">Iqaluit</a> sits created a north flow into <a href="/wiki/Cumberland_Sound" title="Cumberland Sound">Cumberland Sound</a> and a south flow into the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Strait" title="Hudson Strait">Hudson Strait</a>. A secondary Hall Ice Divide formed a link to a local ice cap on the <a href="/wiki/Hall_Peninsula" title="Hall Peninsula">Hall Peninsula</a>. The current ice caps on Baffin Island are thought to be a remnant from this time period, but it was not a part of the Baffin ice flow, but an autonomous flow.<sup id="cite_ref-Dyke1987_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dyke1987-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Adjacent_ice_sheets">Adjacent ice sheets</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laurentide_ice_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Adjacent ice sheets"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Cordilleran_ice_sheet">Cordilleran ice sheet</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laurentide_ice_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cordilleran ice sheet"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cordilleran_ice_sheet" title="Cordilleran ice sheet">Cordilleran ice sheet</a> covered up to 1,500,000 square kilometres (580,000&#160;sq&#160;mi) at the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The eastern edge abutted the Laurentide ice sheet. The sheet was anchored in the <a href="/wiki/Coast_Mountains" title="Coast Mountains">Coast Mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, south into the <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Range" title="Cascade Range">Cascade Range</a> of <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>. That is one and a half times the water held in the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a>. Anchored in the mountain backbone of the west coast, the ice sheet dissipated north of the <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Range" title="Alaska Range">Alaska Range</a> where the air was too dry to form glaciers.<sup id="cite_ref-Hill2006_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hill2006-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
It is believed that the Cordilleran ice melted rapidly, in less than 4000 years. The water created numerous <a href="/wiki/Proglacial_lakes" class="mw-redirect" title="Proglacial lakes">Proglacial lakes</a> along the margins such as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Missoula" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Missoula">Lake Missoula</a>, often leading to catastrophic floods as with the <a href="/wiki/Missoula_Floods" class="mw-redirect" title="Missoula Floods">Missoula Floods</a>. Much of the topography of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Washington" title="Eastern Washington">Eastern Washington</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a> was affected.<sup id="cite_ref-Hill2006_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hill2006-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
