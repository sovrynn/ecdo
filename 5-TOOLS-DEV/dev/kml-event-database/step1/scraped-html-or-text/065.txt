<p class="mw-empty-elt">

</p><p><b>Dvārakā</b>, also known as <b>Dvāravatī</b> (<a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> द्वारका "the gated [city]", possibly meaning having many gates, or alternatively having one or several very grand gates), is a sacred historic city in the sacred literature of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jer_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jer-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Law1941_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Law1941-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jaiswal_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jaiswal-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is also alternatively spelled as <b>Dvarika</b>. The name Dvaraka is said to have been given to the place by <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a>, a major deity in Hinduism.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Dvaraka is one of the <a href="/wiki/Sapta_Puri" title="Sapta Puri">Sapta Puri</a> (seven sacred cities) of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>.
</p><p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></i>, it was a city located in what is now <a href="/wiki/Dwarka" title="Dwarka">Dwarka</a>, formerly called Kushasthali, the fort of which had to be repaired by the <a href="/wiki/Yadava" title="Yadava">Yadavas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In this epic, the city is described as a capital of the <a href="/wiki/Anarta_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Anarta Kingdom">Anarta Kingdom</a>. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Harivamsa" class="mw-redirect" title="Harivamsa">Harivamsa</a></i> the city was located in the region of the <a href="/wiki/Sindhu_Kingdom" title="Sindhu Kingdom">Sindhu Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In the Hindu epics and the <a href="/wiki/Puranas" title="Puranas">Puranas</a>, Dvaraka is called Dvaravati and is one of seven <a href="/wiki/Tirtha_(Hinduism)" title="Tirtha (Hinduism)">Tirtha</a> (pilgrimage) sites for spiritual liberation. The other six are <a href="/wiki/Mathura" title="Mathura">Mathura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayodhya_(Ramayana)" title="Ayodhya (Ramayana)">Ayodhya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Varanasi" title="Varanasi">Kashi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanchipuram" title="Kanchipuram">Kanchipuram</a>, Avantika (<a href="/wiki/Ujjain" title="Ujjain">Ujjain</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Puri" title="Puri">Puri</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

Bhima arriving at the castle of Dvaraka to invite Krishna to the asvamedha. Krishna, who is with his wife Satyabhama, pretends not to hear..</figcaption></figure>
<p>The following description of Dvaraka during <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a>'s presence there appears in the <i><a href="/wiki/Bhagavata_Purana" title="Bhagavata Purana">Bhagavata Purana</a></i> (10.69.1-12) in connection with the sage Narada's visit:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The city was filled with the sounds of birds and bees flying about the parks and pleasure gardens, while its lakes, crowded with blooming indivara, ambhoja, kahlara, kumuda, and utpala lotuses, resounded with the calls of swans and cranes.
</p><p>Dvaraka boasted 900,000 royal palaces, all constructed with crystal and silver and splendorously decorated with huge emeralds. Inside these palaces, the furnishings were bedecked with gold and jewels.
</p><p>Traffic moved along a well laid-out system of boulevards, roads, intersections, and marketplaces, and many assembly houses and temples of demigods graced the charming city. The roads, courtyards, commercial streets, and residential patios were all sprinkled with water and shaded from the sun's heat by banners waving from flagpoles.
</p><p>In the city of Dvaraka was a beautiful private quarter worshiped by the planetary rulers. This district, where the demigod Vishvakarma had shown all his divine skill, was the residential area of Krishna, and thus it was gorgeously decorated by the sixteen thousand palaces of Krishna's queens. Narada entered one of these immense palaces.
</p><p>
Supporting the palace were coral pillars decoratively inlaid with vaidurya gems. Sapphires bedecked the walls, and the floors glowed with perpetual brilliance. In that palace Tvashta had arranged canopies with hanging strands of pearls; there were also seats and beds fashioned of ivory and precious jewels. In attendance were many well-dressed maidservants bearing lockets on their necks, and also armor-clad guards with turbans, fine uniforms, and jeweled earrings.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8202;<cite>Bhagavata Purana, 10.69.1-12</cite></div></blockquote>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Harivamsa">Harivamsa</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dv%C4%81rak%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Harivamsa"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<ul><li>In <i>Harivamsa</i>, Dvaraka is described as largely built on "submerged land", "released by the ocean" (2.55.118 and 2.58.34).</li>
<li>The city was the former "sporting ground of the King Raivataka" called "Dvāravāti", which "was squared like a chess board" (2.56.29).</li>
<li>Nearby was the mountain range <a href="/wiki/Raivataka" title="Raivataka">Raivataka</a> (2.56.27), "the living place of the gods" (2.55.111).</li>
<li>The city was measured by <a href="/wiki/Brahmin" title="Brahmin">Brahmins</a>; the foundations of the houses were laid and at least some of the houses were built by the Yadavas (2.58.9 - 15).</li>
<li>It was built by Vishwakarman in one day (2.58.40) "mentally" (2.58.41 and 44).</li>
<li>It had surrounding walls (2.58.48 and 53) with four main gates (2.58.16).</li>
<li>Its houses were arranged in lines (2.58.41) and the city had "high buildings" (2.58.50 and 54) (2.58.53), which "almost touched the sky" (2.58.50), and had "doors that had the colour of white clouds" (2.58.48).</li>
<li>The fort walls of the city were "shining with the colour of the Sun and pots of gold" and "sounds emanating from grand houses sparkling with golden colour" (2.58.53).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>It had a temple area with a palace for Krishna himself, which had a separate bathroom (2.58.43).</li>
<li>"The city is beautified on Earth by the ocean" like Indra's heavenly city is "beautified by an assembly of important jewels" (2.58.47 - 66, (2.58.49).</li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Events">Events</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dv%C4%81rak%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Events"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Pandu" title="Pandu">Pandu</a>'s sons lived in Dvaraka during their exile to woods. Their servants headed by Indrasena lived there for one year (the 13th year) (4,72).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bala_Rama" class="mw-redirect" title="Bala Rama">Balarama</a> mentioned about a sacrificial fire of Dvaraka, before he set for his pilgrimage over <a href="/wiki/Sarasvati_River" title="Sarasvati River">Sarasvati River</a> (9,35).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rukmini" title="Rukmini">Rukmini</a> is described to become the chief queen of Dvaraka after her elopement with Krishna,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> equated with the goddess <a href="/wiki/Lakshmi" title="Lakshmi">Lakshmi</a> as Krishna's chief consort in the Mahabharata.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>One should proceed with subdued senses and regulated diet to Dvaravati, where by bathing in "the holy place called Pindaraka",<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> one obtaineth the fruit of the gift of gold in abundance (3,82).</li>
<li>King Nriga, in consequence of a single fault of his, had to dwell for a long time at Dvaravati, and <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krishna</a> became the cause of his rescue from that miserable plight.(13,72).</li>
<li>Sage <a href="/wiki/Durvasa" title="Durvasa">Durvasa</a> resided at Dvaravati for a long time (13,160).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arjuna" title="Arjuna">Arjuna</a> visited Dvaravati during his military campaign after the <a href="/wiki/Kurukshetra_War" title="Kurukshetra War">Kurukshetra War</a> (14,83).</li>
<li>When the Pandavas retire from the world they visit the place where Dvaraka once used to be and see the city submerged under water.</li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Related_archaeology">Related archaeology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dv%C4%81rak%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Related archaeology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>During 1983–1990, the Marine Archaeology Unit of India's <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Oceanography,_India" title="National Institute of Oceanography, India">National Institute of Oceanography</a> (NIO) carried out underwater excavations at <a href="/wiki/Dwarka" title="Dwarka">Dwarka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bet_Dwarka" title="Bet Dwarka">Bet Dwarka</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTES._R._Rao199151_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTES._R._Rao199151-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Shikaripura_Ranganatha_Rao" title="Shikaripura Ranganatha Rao">S. R. Rao</a> "The available archaeological evidence from onshore and offshore excavations confirms the existence of a city-state with a couple of satellite towns in 1500 B.C." He considered it reasonable to conclude that this submerged city is the Dvaraka as described in the <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTES._R._Rao199159_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTES._R._Rao199159-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Submergence">Submergence</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dv%C4%81rak%C4%81&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Submergence"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Mausala_Parva" title="Mausala Parva">Mausala Parva</a></i> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Arjuna" title="Arjuna">Arjuna</a> witnesses the submergence of Dvaraka and describes it as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1244412712"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The sea, which had been beating against the shores, suddenly broke the boundary that was imposed on it by nature. The sea rushed into the city. It coursed through the streets of the beautiful city. The sea covered up everything in the city. I saw the beautiful buildings becoming submerged one by one. In a matter of a few moments it was all over. The sea had now become as placid as a lake. There was no trace of the city. Dvaraka was just a name; just a memory.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8202;<cite><i>Mausala Parva</i> of <i>Mahabharata</i></cite></div></blockquote>
