<p>The <b>Sacred City of Caral-Supe</b>, or simply <b>Caral</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_site" title="Archaeological site">archaeological site</a> in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> where the remains of the main city of the <a href="/wiki/Caral_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Caral civilization">Caral civilization</a> are found. It is located in the Supe valley of Peru, near the current town of Caral, 182 kilometers north of <a href="/wiki/Lima" title="Lima">Lima</a>, 23&#160;km from the coast and 350 <a href="/wiki/Metres_above_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Metres above sea level">metres above sea level</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is attributed an antiquity of 5,000 years and it is considered the oldest city in the Americas and one of the oldest in the world. No other site has been found with such a diversity of monumental buildings or different ceremonial and administrative functions in the Americas as early as Caral.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It has been declared a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">Humanity Cultural Heritage</a> site by <a href="/wiki/Unesco" class="mw-redirect" title="Unesco">UNESCO</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The Caral culture developed between 3000 and 1800 B.C (Late Archaic and Lower Formative periods). In America, it is the oldest of the pre-Hispanic civilizations, developing 1,500 years earlier than the <a href="/wiki/Olmec_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Olmec civilization">Olmec civilization</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerican</a> complex society.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Closely related to the city of Caral was an early fishing city, <a href="/wiki/Aspero" title="Aspero">Áspero or El Áspero</a>, located on the coast near the mouth of the Supe River. There, remains of human sacrifices (two children and a newborn) have been found. In 2016, the remains were found of a woman, who presumably belonged to the local elite of 4,500 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Caral was inhabited between approximately <a href="/wiki/26th_century_BC" title="26th century BC">26th century BC</a> and <a href="/wiki/20th_century_BC" title="20th century BC">20th century BC</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and the site includes an area of more than 60 hectares (150 acres).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Caral has been described by its excavators as the oldest urban center in the Americas. This claim has been challenged by the discovery of other ancient sites nearby, such as <a href="/wiki/Bandurria,_Peru" title="Bandurria, Peru">Bandurria, Peru</a>. Accommodating more than 3,000 inhabitants, Caral is the best studied and one of the largest sites known of the <a href="/wiki/Norte_Chico_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Norte Chico civilization">Norte Chico civilization</a>.
</p><p>The city was declared a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-UNESCO_WHC_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO_WHC-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In early 2021, tensions arose between squatters claiming land rights and archaeologists researching the site, as housing construction encroached on the site.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Archaeological_findings">Archaeological findings</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Archaeological findings"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paul_Kosok" title="Paul Kosok">Paul Kosok</a> discovered Caral in 1948.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The site received little attention at the time because it appeared to lack many of the typical artifacts that were sought at archaeological sites throughout the Andes.
</p><p>In 1975, the Peruvian architect Carlos Williams made a detailed record of most of the archaeological sites of the valley of Supe, among which he recorded Caral. Based on what he observed in the region, he made some observations about the development of architecture in the Andes.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ruth_Shady" title="Ruth Shady">Ruth Shady</a> further explored this 4,000- to 4,600-year-old city in the Peruvian desert, with its elaborate complex of temples, an amphitheater, and ordinary houses.<sup id="cite_ref-Shady01_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shady01-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The urban complex is spread out over 150 hectares (370 acres) and contains plazas and residential buildings. Caral was a thriving metropolis at roughly the same time as the great pyramids were being built in Egypt, which is considered one of the earliest civilizations in the world.
</p><p>Caral is the largest recorded site in the Andean region, with dates older than 2000 BC. It appears to be the model for the urban design adopted by Andean civilizations that rose and fell over the span of four millennia. Scholars believe that research conducted in Caral may answer questions about the origins of the <a href="/wiki/Andean_civilisations" class="mw-redirect" title="Andean civilisations">Andean civilizations</a> and the development of its first cities.
</p>

<p>Among the artifacts found at Caral is a knotted textile piece that the excavators have labelled a <i><a href="/wiki/Quipu" title="Quipu">quipu</a></i>. They write that the artifact is evidence that the <i>quipu</i> record keeping system, a method involving knots tied in textiles that was brought to its highest development by the <a href="/wiki/Inca_Empire" title="Inca Empire">Inca Empire</a>, was older than any archaeologist previously had determined. Evidence has emerged that the <i>quipu</i> also may have recorded <a href="/wiki/Logograph" class="mw-redirect" title="Logograph">logographic</a> information in the same way writing does. <a href="/wiki/Gary_Urton" title="Gary Urton">Gary Urton</a> has suggested that the <i>quipus</i> used a <a href="/wiki/Binary_code" title="Binary code">binary code</a> that could record <a href="/wiki/Phonological" class="mw-redirect" title="Phonological">phonological</a> or logographic data.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Main_temple">Main temple</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Main temple"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The main temple complex (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">Templo Mayor</i>) is 150 meters (490&#160;ft) long, 110 meters (360&#160;ft) wide and 28 meters (92&#160;ft) high. The date of its construction is unknown.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Peaceful_society">Peaceful society</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Peaceful society"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Shady's findings suggest it was a society built on commerce and pleasure. No indications of warfare, such as battlements, weapons, or mutilated bodies, have been found at Caral. This contrasts with the older site of <a href="/wiki/Sechin_Bajo" title="Sechin Bajo">Sechin Bajo</a>, where depictions of weapons are found. In one of the Caral temples, researchers found 32 flutes made of <a href="/wiki/Condor" title="Condor">condor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pelican" title="Pelican">pelican</a> bones and 37 <a href="/wiki/Cornett" title="Cornett">cornetts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Llama" title="Llama">llama</a> bones. One find revealed the remains of a baby, wrapped and buried with a necklace made of stone beads.<sup id="cite_ref-Shady01_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shady01-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Scope_of_site">Scope of site</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scope of site"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Caral was flanked by 19 other <a href="/wiki/Temple" title="Temple">temple</a> complexes scattered across the 90 square kilometres (35&#160;sq&#160;mi) area of the Supe Valley.
</p><p>The date of 2627 BC for Caral is based on the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dating" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon dating">carbon dating</a> of reed and woven carrying bags that were found on site. These bags were used to carry the stones for the construction of the temples. The material is an excellent candidate for high precision dating. The site may date even earlier, however, as samples from the oldest parts of the excavation have yet to be dated.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Caral had a population of approximately 3,000 people. However, 19 other sites in the area (posted at Caral), allow for a possible total population of 20,000 people sharing the same culture in the Supe Valley. All of these sites share similarities with Caral, including small platforms or stone circles. Shady believes that Caral was the focus of this civilization. It is believed to be part of an even more vast cultural complex, trading with the coastal communities and the regions farther inland. She suggests that a depiction of monkeys may be evidence of trade with communities along the Amazon.<sup id="cite_ref-Shady01_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shady01-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Geoglyph">Geoglyph</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geoglyph"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In 2000, Marco Machacuay (the chief of excavations at the time) and his colleague, Rocío Aramburú, discovered a large shape etched on the ground among circular stone lines near Caral. This image, known as a <a href="/wiki/Geoglyph" title="Geoglyph">geoglyph</a>, is located on the desert floor just west of the main site at Caral. The lines form the design of a human face with long, streaming hair and a gaping mouth.
</p><p>This geoglyph is similar to bleeding figures with a similar gaping mouths found etched onto the stone walls at a site called <a href="/wiki/Cerro_Sech%C3%ADn" title="Cerro Sechín">Cerro Sechín</a>, in the Casma Valley 240 kilometres (150&#160;mi) to the north. What this figure represents is unclear. It is believed to have been constructed around the same time as Caral and to have been associated with a nearby ceremonial site known as <i>Chupacigarro</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Musical_instruments">Musical instruments</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Musical instruments"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Another significant find at the site was a collection of musical instruments, including 37 <a href="/wiki/Cornett" title="Cornett">cornetts</a> made of deer and llama bones and 33 flutes of unusual construction.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The flutes were radiocarbon dated to 2170±90 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-Shady01_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shady01-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="City_layout">City layout</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caral&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: City layout"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The city of Caral was split into two sections, an "Upper Half" and a "Lower Half". These halves were divided naturally by the Supe River Valley.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the Upper Half there are six monumental complexes, each of which includes a pyramid, open plaza, and assemblage of residential buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Shady01_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shady01-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the Lower Half there are residential buildings, small pyramids, and one monumental complex called the "Temple of the Amphitheater".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The Upper Half complexes were all constructed around a pyramid. These are the "Great Temple/Great Pyramid", "Central Pyramid", "Quarry Pyramid", "Lesser Pyramid", "Pyramid of the Gallery", and "Pyramid of the Huanca".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The associated residential structures around each of these pyramids contain evidence of elite living, including food remnants that would have been exclusive to elite lifestyles, such as sea lion bones.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the Upper Half of Caral, many of the residents were wealthy elites, whose lives likely were associated with religious and social activities that would have taken place in the temples.<sup id="cite_ref-Shady01_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shady01-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>By comparison, the residential buildings in the Lower Half have less evidence of elite populations. Instead of the large structures, exclusively elite residential complexes of the Upper Half, these residences are smaller and single rooms are used for more than one purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The diets of the people living in the Lower Half of Caral consisted mostly of agricultural plants and some fish.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These diets were less rich than those of the elites living in the Upper Half.
</p><p>The current explanation for the divided city is that the city was intentionally planned in this way, with the monumental architecture and complexes of the Upper Half designed both to house elites and to physically indicate their political power.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Conversely, the Lower Half was designed to house laborers, with the river serving as the division between these groups. This sort of intentional city planning is evidence of structuralized inequality at Caral, which perpetuated existing social stratification.<sup id="cite_ref-Shady01_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shady01-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
