					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Hypothetical flood scenario</div>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_the_Black_Sea_with_bathymetry_and_surrounding_relief.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Map_of_the_Black_Sea_with_bathymetry_and_surrounding_relief.svg/300px-Map_of_the_Black_Sea_with_bathymetry_and_surrounding_relief.svg.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="190" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Map_of_the_Black_Sea_with_bathymetry_and_surrounding_relief.svg/450px-Map_of_the_Black_Sea_with_bathymetry_and_surrounding_relief.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Map_of_the_Black_Sea_with_bathymetry_and_surrounding_relief.svg/600px-Map_of_the_Black_Sea_with_bathymetry_and_surrounding_relief.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1013" data-file-height="643" /></a><figcaption>Map of the Black Sea</figcaption></figure>
<p>The <b>Black Sea deluge</b> is the best known of three hypothetical flood scenarios proposed for the Late <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a> history of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. One other flood scenario proposes a rapid, even catastrophic, rise in sea level of the Black Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-Yanko-HombachOthers2011a_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yanko-HombachOthers2011a-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson2012a_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson2012a-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Sea_deluge_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Black-sea-hist.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Black-sea-hist.png/220px-Black-sea-hist.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/31/Black-sea-hist.png 1.5x" data-file-width="313" data-file-height="199" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> today (light blue) and in 7550 <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">YBP</a> (dark blue) according to the hypothesis by Ryan and Pitman</figcaption></figure>
<p>In 1997, <a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Ryan_(geologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="William Ryan (geologist) (page does not exist)">William Ryan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Pitman_(geologist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Walter Pitman (geologist)">Walter Pitman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petko_Dimitrov" title="Petko Dimitrov">Petko Dimitrov</a>, and their colleagues first published the <i>Black Sea deluge hypothesis</i>. They proposed that a catastrophic inflow of <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> into the Black Sea <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> occurred around 7,600 years ago, <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;5600 BCE</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RyanOthers1997a_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RyanOthers1997a-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>As proposed, the Early Holocene Black Sea flood scenario describes events that would have profoundly affected prehistoric settlement in Eastern Europe and adjacent parts of Asia and possibly was the basis of oral history concerning the myth of <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_flood" class="mw-redirect" title="Noah&#39;s flood">Noah's flood</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RyanOthers1997a_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RyanOthers1997a-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Some archaeologists support this theory as an explanation for the lack of <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Neolithic period">Neolithic</a> sites in northern Turkey.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2003, Ryan and coauthors revised the dating of the early Holocene flood to 8,800 years ago, <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;6800 BCE</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-RyanOthers2003a_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RyanOthers2003a-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Before that date, <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacial</a> <a href="/wiki/Meltwater" title="Meltwater">meltwater</a> had turned the Black and <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian seas</a> into vast freshwater lakes draining into the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a>. As glaciers retreated, some of the rivers emptying into the Black Sea declined in volume and changed course to drain into the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>. The levels of the lakes dropped through evaporation, while changes in worldwide <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a> caused global sea levels to rise.<sup id="cite_ref-RyanOthers2003a_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RyanOthers2003a-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ryan1998a_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan1998a-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The rising <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> finally spilled over a rocky <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">sill</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Bosporus" title="Bosporus">Bosporus</a>. The event flooded 100,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (39,000&#160;sq&#160;mi) of land and significantly expanded the Black Sea shoreline to the north and west. According to these researchers, 50&#160;km<sup>3</sup> (10&#160;cu&#160;mi) of water poured through each day. The Bosporus valley roared and surged at full spate for at least 300 days. They argued that the catastrophic inflow of seawater resulted from an abrupt sea-level jump that accompanied the <a href="/wiki/Laurentide_Ice_Sheet" class="mw-redirect" title="Laurentide Ice Sheet">Laurentide Ice Sheet</a> collapse and the ensuing breach of a bedrock barrier in the Bosporus strait.<sup id="cite_ref-RyanOthers2003a_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RyanOthers2003a-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ryan1998a_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan1998a-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Popular_press_accounts">Popular press accounts</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Sea_deluge_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Popular press accounts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Popular discussion of this early Holocene Black Sea flood scenario was headlined in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> in December 1996<sup id="cite_ref-WilfordOthers1996_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WilfordOthers1996-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and later published as a book.<sup id="cite_ref-Ryan1998a_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ryan1998a-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  In a series of expeditions widely covered by mainstream media, a team of marine archaeologists led by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ballard" title="Robert Ballard">Robert Ballard</a> identified what appeared to be ancient shorelines, freshwater snail shells, drowned river valleys, tool-worked timbers, and man-made structures in roughly 100 metres (330&#160;ft) of water off the Black Sea coast of modern Turkey.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Late_Pleistocene_Great_Flood_hypothesis">Late Pleistocene Great Flood hypothesis</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Sea_deluge_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Late Pleistocene Great Flood hypothesis"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In 2003 and 2007, a more ancient catastrophic flood scenario was proposed by Andrei L. Chepalyga for the Late Quaternary sea level rise of the Black Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-Yanko-HombachOthers2011a_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yanko-HombachOthers2011a-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson2012a_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson2012a-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chepalyga2003a_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chepalyga2003a-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The hypothesis for a <b>Late Pleistocene Great Flood</b> argues that brackish Neoeuxinian Lake, which occupied the Black Sea basin, was rapidly inundated by glacial meltwater overflow from the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Manych_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Manych River">Manych</a>-<a href="/wiki/Kerch_Strait" title="Kerch Strait">Kerch</a> Spillway shortly after the <a href="/wiki/Late_Glacial_Maximum" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Glacial Maximum">Late Glacial Maximum</a>, about 17,000–14,000 BP. These extensive meltwater flooding events linked several <a href="/wiki/Lacustrine" class="mw-redirect" title="Lacustrine">lacustrine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marine_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine water">marine water</a> bodies, starting with the southern edge of the Scandinavian and southward, through spillways to the Manych-Kerch and Bosphorus, ultimately forming what has been referred to as the Cascade of Eurasian Basins.<sup id="cite_ref-Chepalyga2007a_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chepalyga2007a-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This event is argued to have caused a rapid, if not catastrophic, rise in the level of the Black Sea. It might have imposed substantial stresses upon contemporary human populations and remained in cultural memory as the <i>Great Flood</i>. The authors also suggested that the event might have stimulated the beginning of shipping and horse domestication.<sup id="cite_ref-Yanko-HombachOthers2011a_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yanko-HombachOthers2011a-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chepalyga2007a_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chepalyga2007a-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Black_Sea_gradual_inundation_hypothesis">Black Sea gradual inundation hypothesis</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Sea_deluge_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Black Sea gradual inundation hypothesis"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In addition to the early Holocene "Noah's Flood" scenario proposed by Ryan, Pitman, Dimitrov, and their colleagues<sup id="cite_ref-RyanOthers1997a_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RyanOthers1997a-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RyanOthers2003a_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RyanOthers2003a-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and the Caspian Sea overflow scenario of Chepalyga,<sup id="cite_ref-Chepalyga2003a_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chepalyga2003a-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chepalyga2007a_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chepalyga2007a-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> the non-catastrophic <i>progressive flood model</i> (or <i>gradual inflow model</i>) has been proposed to explain the Late Quaternary sea level history of the Black Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferguson2012a_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferguson2012a-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FergusonOther_2012a_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FergusonOther_2012a-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>About 8,000&#160;<a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">YBP</a>, the level of the <a href="/wiki/Marmara_Sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Marmara Sea">Marmara Sea</a> would have risen high enough for two-way flow to start. The evidence used to support this scenario includes the disparate ages of <a href="/wiki/Sapropel" title="Sapropel">sapropel</a> deposition in the eastern Mediterranean Sea and Black Sea; buried back-stepping barrier islands observed on the Black Sea shelf; and an under-water delta in the Marmara Sea, near the Bosporus Strait, composed of Black Sea sediments.<sup id="cite_ref-AksuOther_2012a_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AksuOther_2012a-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AksuOther_2002b_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AksuOther_2002b-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HiscottOther_2007a_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HiscottOther_2007a-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Counter_arguments">Counter arguments</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Sea_deluge_hypothesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Counter arguments"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png/290px-Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png" decoding="async" width="290" height="174" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png/435px-Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png/580px-Post-Glacial_Sea_Level.png 2x" data-file-width="1813" data-file-height="1088" /></a><figcaption>The Post-Glacial Sea Level.</figcaption></figure> 
<p>Criticisms of the deluge hypothesis focus on the magnitude and pace of the water level rise in the Black Sea. With enough moderation of these features, the catastrophe hypothesis is voided. However, a few key points should be noted:
</p>
<ul><li>Since the ending of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>, the global <a href="/wiki/Early_Holocene_sea_level_rise" title="Early Holocene sea level rise">sea level has risen</a> some 120&#160;m (390&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The flood hypothesis hinges on the <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bosporus" title="Bosporus">Bosporus</a> since the end of the glacial age.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Black Sea area has been isolated and reconnected many times during the last 500,000&#160;years.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Opponents of the deluge hypothesis point to clues that water was flowing out of the Black Sea basin as late as 15,000&#160;years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In this alternative scenario, much depends on the evolution of the Bosphorus. According to a study from 2001, the modern sill is 32–34&#160;m (105–112&#160;ft) below sea level and consists of Quaternary sand over-lying Paleozoic bedrock in which three sills are found at 80–85&#160;m (260–280&#160;ft) below sea level. <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">Sedimentation</a> on these sills started before 10,000 years ago and continued until 5,300 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A large part of the academic geological community also continues to reject the idea that there could have been enough sustained long-term pressure by water from the Aegean to dig through a supposed isthmus at the present Bosphorus or enough of a difference in water levels, if at all, between the two water basins.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007, a research anthology on the topic was published which makes much of the earlier Russian research available in English for the first time and combines it with more recent scientific findings.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The level in the Black Sea before the marine reconnection was estimated to have been 30&#160;m (100&#160;ft) below present sea level, rather than 80&#160;m (260&#160;ft) of the catastrophe theories or even lower; if the flood occurred at all, the sea level increase and the flooded area during the reconnection were significantly smaller than previously proposed. Since the depth of the Bosphorus, in its middle furrow, at present varies from 36 to 124&#160;m (118 to 407&#160;ft), with an average depth of 65&#160;m (213&#160;ft), a calculated <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a> shoreline in the Black Sea lying 30&#160;m (100&#160;ft) lower than in the present day would imply that the contact with the Mediterranean might never have been broken during the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>, and hence there could have been no sudden waterfall-style transgression.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The flooding could have been "not so big".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, several authors concluded that "there is no underwater archaeological evidence to support any catastrophic submergence of prehistoric Black Sea settlements during the late Pleistocene or early Holocene intervals".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A 2012 study based on <a href="/wiki/Process_(anatomy)" title="Process (anatomy)">process</a> length variation of the <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellate_cyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Dinoflagellate cyst">dinoflagellate cyst</a> <i>Lingulodinium machaerophorum</i> shows no evidence for catastrophic flooding.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
<a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">Geophysical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geochronology" title="Geochronology">geochronological</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geochemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Geochemical">geochemical</a> evidence points to a "fast transgression" of the submergence lasting between 10 and 200 years.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A 2022 literature review concluded that there was insufficient evidence for a flood scenario. It was more likely that the waters of the Black Sea <i>itself</i> gradually outflowed to the Mediterranean. There was also no archaeological evidence of humans evacuating the area during the relevant time frame.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
