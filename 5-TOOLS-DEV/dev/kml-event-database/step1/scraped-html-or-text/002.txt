<p><b>Lyuba</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Люба</span>) is a female <a href="/wiki/Woolly_mammoth" title="Woolly mammoth">woolly mammoth</a> calf (<i>Mammuthus primigenius</i>) who died <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 42,000 years ago<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_et_al.,_2012_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher_et_al.,_2012-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kosintsev_et_al.,_2012_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kosintsev_et_al.,_2012-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> at the age of 30 to 35 days.<sup id="cite_ref-Rountrey_et_al.,_2012_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rountrey_et_al.,_2012-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> She was formerly the best preserved mammoth mummy in the world (the distinction is now held by <a href="/wiki/Yuka_(mammoth)" title="Yuka (mammoth)">Yuka</a>), surpassing Dima, a male mammoth calf mummy which had previously been the best known specimen.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Discovery">Discovery</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lyuba_(mammoth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Lyuba was discovered in May 2007 by <a href="/wiki/Nenets_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Nenets people">Nenets</a> reindeer breeder and hunter Yuri Khudi and his three sons, in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>'s Arctic <a href="/wiki/Yamal_Peninsula" title="Yamal Peninsula">Yamal Peninsula</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hirsch2017_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch2017-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Khudi recognized that Lyuba was a mammoth carcass and that it was an important find, but refused to touch the carcass because Nenets beliefs associated touching mammoth remains with bad omens.<sup id="cite_ref-NG200905icebaby_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NG200905icebaby-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Khudi travelled to a small town 150 miles away to consult his friend, Kirill Serotetto, on how to proceed. They notified the local museum director about the find, who arranged the authorities to fly Serotetto and Khudi back to the location of the find on the <a href="/wiki/Yuribey_Bridge" title="Yuribey Bridge">Yuribey</a> river. However, they found that Lyuba's remains had disappeared. Suspecting that profiteers may have taken the mammoth, Khudi and Serotetto drove on a snowmobile to a nearby settlement, <a href="/wiki/Novy_Port" title="Novy Port">Novy Port</a>. There they discovered Lyuba's carcass exhibited outside a local store. It turned out that the store owner bought the body from Khudi's cousin, who removed the body from its original location, in exchange for two snowmobiles. Lyuba's body suffered minor damage in the process, with dogs having chewed off her right ear and a part of her tail, but remained largely intact. With the help of the police, Khudi and Serotetto reclaimed the body and had it transported by helicopter to the Shemanovsky Museum in <a href="/wiki/Salekhard" title="Salekhard">Salekhard</a>. In gratitude for Khudi's role, the museum officials named the mammoth calf "<a href="/wiki/Lyuba_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lyuba (given name)">Lyuba</a>", a diminutive form of the name <i>Lyubov</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span> (Любовь, meaning "Love"), after the first name of Khudi's wife.<sup id="cite_ref-NG200905icebaby_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NG200905icebaby-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hirsch2017_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirsch2017-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Subsequent_study">Subsequent study</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lyuba_(mammoth)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subsequent study"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The mummified calf weighed 50&#160;kg (110&#160;lb), was 85 centimeters (33.5 in.) high and measured 130 centimeters (51 in.) from trunk to tail, roughly the same size as a large dog.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC-Mammoth-2007_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-Mammoth-2007-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reuters-Mammoth-2007_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-Mammoth-2007-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Studies of her teeth indicate she was born in spring following a gestation similar in length to that of a modern elephant.<sup id="cite_ref-Rountrey_et_al.,_2012_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rountrey_et_al.,_2012-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<p>At the time of discovery, the calf was remarkably well-preserved; her eyes and trunk were intact and some fur remained on her body. Lyuba's organs and skin are in perfect condition.<sup id="cite_ref-usatoday20090421_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday20090421-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The mammoth was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Jikei_University_School_of_Medicine" title="Jikei University School of Medicine">Jikei University School of Medicine</a> in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> for further study, including <a href="/wiki/Computer_tomography" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer tomography">computer tomography</a> scans. Additional scans were conducted at the GE Healthcare Institute in <a href="/wiki/Waukesha,_Wisconsin" title="Waukesha, Wisconsin">Waukesha, Wisconsin</a> and at the Nondestructive Evaluation Laboratory of Ford Motor Company in <a href="/wiki/Livonia,_Michigan" title="Livonia, Michigan">Livonia, Michigan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_et_al.,_2014_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher_et_al.,_2014-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Lyuba is believed to have suffocated by inhaling mud as she struggled while bogged down in deep mud<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_et_al.,_2012_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher_et_al.,_2012-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> in the bed of a river which her herd was crossing. Following death, her body may have been colonized by lactic acid-producing bacteria, which "pickled" her,<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_et_al.,_2012_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher_et_al.,_2012-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> preserving the mammoth in a nearly pristine state. Her skin and organs are intact, and scientists were able to identify milk from her mother in her stomach, and fecal matter in her intestine.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_et_al.,_2012_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher_et_al.,_2012-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-van_Geel_et_al.,_2011_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-van_Geel_et_al.,_2011-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The fecal matter may have been eaten by Lyuba to promote development of the intestinal microbial assemblage necessary for digestion of vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_et_al.,_2012_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher_et_al.,_2012-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Lyuba appears to have been healthy at the time of her death. By examining Lyuba's teeth, researchers hope to gain insight into what caused Ice Age mammals, including the mammoths, to <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_extinction_event" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaternary extinction event">become extinct</a> 4,500–4,000 years ago. CT scans taken of Lyuba have provided new information and indicate that the mammoth died when she inhaled mud and choked to death.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Lyuba's permanent home is the Shemanovskiy Museum and Exhibition Center in <a href="/wiki/Salekhard" title="Salekhard">Salekhard</a>, Russia.<sup id="cite_ref-usatoday20100308_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday20100308-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Lyuba was the subject of a 2009 documentary <i>Waking the Baby Mammoth</i> by the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Channel" class="mw-redirect" title="National Geographic Channel">National Geographic Channel</a><sup id="cite_ref-usatoday20090421_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday20090421-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and of a 2011 children's book by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_P._Sloan" title="Christopher P. Sloan">Christopher Sloan</a>, <i>Baby Mammoth Mummy: Frozen in Time: A Prehistoric Animal's Journey into the 21st Century</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
