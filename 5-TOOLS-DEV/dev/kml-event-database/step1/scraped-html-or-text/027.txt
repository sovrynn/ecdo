					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Prehistoric landslide off Norway</div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:StoreggaFLCommonsZone.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/StoreggaFLCommonsZone.jpg/220px-StoreggaFLCommonsZone.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="230" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/StoreggaFLCommonsZone.jpg/330px-StoreggaFLCommonsZone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/StoreggaFLCommonsZone.jpg/440px-StoreggaFLCommonsZone.jpg 2x" data-file-width="687" data-file-height="717" /></a><figcaption>Map of <i>Storegga Slides</i></figcaption></figure>
<p>The three <b>Storegga Slides</b> (<a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>: <i lang="no">Storeggaraset</i>) are amongst the largest known <a href="/wiki/Submarine_landslide" title="Submarine landslide">submarine landslides</a>. They occurred at the edge of Norway's <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">Norwegian Sea</a>, approximately 6225–6170 <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">BCE</a>. The collapse involved an estimated 290&#160;km (180&#160;mi) length of coastal shelf, with a total volume of 3,500&#160;km<sup>3</sup> (840&#160;cu&#160;mi) of debris, which caused a <a href="/wiki/Paleotsunami" title="Paleotsunami">paleotsunami</a> in the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Description">Description</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storegga_Slide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:StoreggaFLCommonsZone2OK.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/StoreggaFLCommonsZone2OK.jpg/220px-StoreggaFLCommonsZone2OK.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="162" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/StoreggaFLCommonsZone2OK.jpg/330px-StoreggaFLCommonsZone2OK.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/StoreggaFLCommonsZone2OK.jpg/440px-StoreggaFLCommonsZone2OK.jpg 2x" data-file-width="617" data-file-height="454" /></a><figcaption>The yellow numbers give the height of the tsunami wave as indicated by <a href="/wiki/Tsunamite" class="mw-redirect" title="Tsunamite">tsunamites</a> studied by researchers.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<p>Storegga (<a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>: <i>Great Edge</i>) is located at the edge of Norway's continental shelf in the Norwegian Sea, 100&#160;km (62&#160;mi) north-west of the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B8re_og_Romsdal" title="Møre og Romsdal">Møre</a> coast. In around 6200 BCE, structural failures of the shelf caused three underwater <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a>, which triggered very large <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunamis</a> in the North Atlantic Ocean. The collapses involved an estimated 290&#160;km (180&#160;mi) length of coastal shelf, with a total volume of 3,500&#160;km<sup>3</sup> (840&#160;cu&#160;mi) of debris.<sup id="cite_ref-stein_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stein-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p><p>Based on <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dating" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon dating">carbon dating</a> of plant material recovered from sediment deposited by the tsunamis, the latest incident occurred around 6225–6170 BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, traces of the subsequent tsunami have been recorded, with deposited sediment being discovered in <a href="/wiki/Montrose_Basin" title="Montrose Basin">Montrose Basin</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Forth" title="Firth of Forth">Firth of Forth</a> up to 29&#160;km (18&#160;mi) inland and 4&#160;m (13&#160;ft) above current normal tide levels.<sup id="cite_ref-brooks_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brooks-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Possible_mechanism">Possible mechanism</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storegga_Slide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Possible mechanism"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The triggering mechanism is thought to have been an earthquake that induced a catastrophic expansion of <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">methane clathrate</a>, a solid compound consisting of large amounts of methane suspended within a crystal water structure that forms in deep oceans under extremely high pressure. If removed from a high-pressure, low-temperature environment, one cubic metre of solid methane clathrate expands to 164 cubic metres of gaseous methane.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> If such an expansion occurred, it may have weakened the integrity of the surrounding rock sufficiently to trigger the slide.
</p><p>A second theory states that over time, streams from melting glaciers had carried trillions of tons of sediment to the edge of the continental shelf, where it accumulated in many layers. In this case, a trigger such as an earthquake could have caused a large area of seafloor to collapse into the deep Norwegian sea, thus carrying the enormous volume of accumulated sediment along with it.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Impact_on_human_populations">Impact on human populations</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storegga_Slide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact on human populations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Storegga_tsunami_deposits,_Montrose_basin_(Maryton).jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Storegga_tsunami_deposits%2C_Montrose_basin_%28Maryton%29.jpg/220px-Storegga_tsunami_deposits%2C_Montrose_basin_%28Maryton%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Storegga_tsunami_deposits%2C_Montrose_basin_%28Maryton%29.jpg/330px-Storegga_tsunami_deposits%2C_Montrose_basin_%28Maryton%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Storegga_tsunami_deposits%2C_Montrose_basin_%28Maryton%29.jpg/440px-Storegga_tsunami_deposits%2C_Montrose_basin_%28Maryton%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a><figcaption>Storegga tsunami deposits (grey upper layer), bracketed by peat (dark brown layers), taken at Maryton on the Montrose Basin, Scotland</figcaption></figure>
<p>At, or shortly before, the time of the Second Storegga Slide, a <a href="/wiki/Land_bridge" title="Land bridge">land bridge</a> known to archaeologists and geologists as <a href="/wiki/Doggerland" title="Doggerland">Doggerland</a> linked <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> across what is now the southern <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>.  This area is believed to have included a coastline of lagoons, marshes, mudflats and beaches, and to have been a rich hunting, fowling and fishing ground populated by <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> human cultures.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gaffney_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gaffney-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Although Doggerland was permanently submerged through a gradual rise in sea level, it has been hypothesized that coastal areas of both Britain and mainland Europe, extending over areas which are now submerged, would have been temporarily inundated by a tsunami triggered by the Storegga Slide.  This event would have had a catastrophic impact on the Mesolithic population at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HillCollins2014_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HillCollins2014-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is estimated that up to a quarter of the Mesolithic population of Britain lost their lives.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A 2021 study found that about 600&#160;km (370 miles) of Scotland's northern and eastern coastline were affected, with water encroaching 29&#160;km (18 miles) inland. With present-day populations and sea levels, a similar event today could devastate and destroy seafront and port areas of Arbroath, Stonehaven, Aberdeen, Inverness, Wick, and Montrose.<sup id="cite_ref-brooks_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brooks-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>While the tsunami caused by the Second Storegga Slide would have been devastating for those within the run-in zone, ultimately the tsunami was neither universally catastrophic nor the reason behind the inundation of the last vestiges of Doggerland.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Future_slides">Future slides</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storegga_Slide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Future slides"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Storegga has been thoroughly investigated as part of the preparation activities for the <a href="/wiki/Ormen_Lange_(gas_field)" title="Ormen Lange (gas field)">Ormen Lange gas field</a> off the coast of Norway. The prevalent conclusion is that the slide was caused by glacial deposits left behind after the previous <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">glacial period</a>, making any recurrence only possible following a new glaciation.<sup id="cite_ref-stein_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stein-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After facts and arguments supporting this conclusion were published in 2004, the development of the Ormen Lange gas field was considered unlikely to increase the risk of triggering a new slide.<sup id="cite_ref-stein_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stein-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
