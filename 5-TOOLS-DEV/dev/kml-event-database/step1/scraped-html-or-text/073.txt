<p><b>Nüwa</b>, also read <b>Nügua</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Mother_goddess" title="Mother goddess">mother goddess</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture_hero" title="Culture hero">culture hero</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:4_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and/or member of the <a href="/wiki/Three_Sovereigns_and_Five_Emperors" title="Three Sovereigns and Five Emperors">Three Sovereigns</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mythology" title="Chinese mythology">Chinese mythology</a>. She is a goddess in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_folk_religion" title="Chinese folk religion">Chinese folk religion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Buddhism" title="Chinese Buddhism">Chinese Buddhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucianism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> She is credited with <a href="/wiki/Creator_deity" title="Creator deity">creating</a> humanity and repairing the Pillar of <a href="/wiki/Tian" title="Tian">Heaven</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>As creator of mankind, she molded humans individually by hand with yellow clay.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In other stories where she fulfills this role, she only created <a href="/wiki/Nobles" class="mw-redirect" title="Nobles">nobles</a><sup id="cite_ref-:024_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:024-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and/or the rich<sup id="cite_ref-:10027_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10027-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> out of yellow soil. The stories vary on the other details about humanity's creation, but it was a tradition commonly believed in ancient China that she created commoners from brown mud.<sup id="cite_ref-:024_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:024-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A story holds that she was tired when she created "the rich and the noble", so all others, or "cord-made people", were created from her "dragg[ing] a string through mud".<sup id="cite_ref-:10027_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10027-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Huainanzi" title="Huainanzi">Huainanzi</a></i>, there is a description of a great battle between deities that broke the pillars supporting Heaven and caused great devastation. There was great flooding, and Heaven had collapsed. Nüwa was the one who patched the holes in Heaven with five colored stones, and she used the legs of a tortoise to mend the pillars.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>There are many instances of her in literature across China which detail her in creation stories, and today, she remains a figure important to Chinese culture. She is one of the most venerated Chinese goddesses alongside <a href="/wiki/Guanyin" title="Guanyin">Guanyin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mazu" title="Mazu">Mazu</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2024)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mythology" title="Chinese mythology">Chinese mythology</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Goddess" title="Goddess">goddess</a> Nüwa is a legendary <a href="/wiki/Progenitor" title="Progenitor">progenitor</a> of all human beings. She also creates a <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Magic (paranormal)">magic</a> stone.<sup id="cite_ref-Gu2006_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gu2006-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Her husband <a href="/wiki/Fu_Xi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fu Xi">Fu Xi</a> is suggested to be the progenitor of <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a> and the patron saint of <a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">numbers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewis2006_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis2006-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Name">Name</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%BCwa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The character <i>nü</i> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">女</span>; <abbr title="Literal translation"><small>lit.</small></abbr> &#39;female&#39;) is a common prefix on the names of goddesses. The proper name is <i>wa</i>, also read as <i>gua</i> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">媧</span>). The Chinese character is unique to this name. Birrell translates it as 'lovely', but notes that it "could be construed as 'frog<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>", which is consistent with her aquatic myth.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In Chinese, the word for 'whirlpool' is <i>wo</i> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">渦</span>), which shares the same pronunciation with the word for 'snail' (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">蝸</span>). These characters all have their right side constructed by the word <i>wa</i> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">咼</span>), which can be translated as 'spiral' or 'helix' as noun, and as 'spin' or 'rotate' when as verb, to describe the 'helical movement'. This mythical meaning has also been symbolically pictured as compasses in the hand which can be found on many paintings and portraits associated with her.
</p><p>Her reverential name is <b>Wahuang</b> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">媧皇</span>; <abbr title="Literal translation"><small>lit.</small></abbr> &#39;Empress Wa&#39;).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Description">Description</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%BCwa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>

<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Huainanzi" title="Huainanzi">Huainanzi</a></i> relates Nüwa to the time when Heaven and Earth were in disruption:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Going back to more ancient times, the four pillars were broken; the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Provinces_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Nine Provinces of China">nine provinces</a> were in tatters. <a href="/wiki/Tian" title="Tian">Heaven</a> did not completely cover [the earth]; Earth did not hold up <a href="/wiki/Tian" title="Tian">[Heaven]</a> all the way around [its circumference]. Fires blazed out of control and could not be extinguished; water flooded in great expanses and would not recede. Ferocious animals ate blameless people; predatory birds snatched the elderly and the weak. Thereupon, Nüwa smelted together five-colored stones in order to patch up the <a href="/wiki/Qing_(color)" class="mw-redirect" title="Qing (color)">azure</a> sky, cut off the legs of <a href="/wiki/Ao_(turtle)" title="Ao (turtle)">the great turtle</a> to set them up as the four pillars, killed the black dragon to provide relief for <a href="/wiki/Ji_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Ji Province">Ji Province</a>, and piled up reeds and cinders to stop the surging waters. The <a href="/wiki/Qing_(color)" class="mw-redirect" title="Qing (color)">azure</a> sky was patched; the four pillars were set up; the surging waters were drained; the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Provinces_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Nine Provinces of China">province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ji_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Ji Province">Ji</a> was tranquil; crafty vermin died off; blameless people [preserved their] lives.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMajor_&amp;_al.2010ch._6_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMajor_&amp;_al.2010ch._6-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>a<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> </p></blockquote>
<p>The catastrophes were supposedly caused by the battle between the deities <a href="/wiki/Gonggong" title="Gonggong">Gonggong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhuanxu" title="Zhuanxu">Zhuanxu</a> (an event that was mentioned earlier in the <i>Huainanzi</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>b<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> the five-colored stones symbolize the <a href="/wiki/Wuxing_(Chinese_philosophy)" title="Wuxing (Chinese philosophy)">five Chinese elements</a> (wood, fire, earth, metal, and water), the black dragon was the essence of water and thus cause of the floods, <a href="/wiki/Ji_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Ji Province">Ji Province</a> serves metonymically for the <a href="/wiki/Zhongyuan" title="Zhongyuan">central regions</a> (the Sinitic world).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMajor_&amp;_al.2010ch._6_n_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMajor_&amp;_al.2010ch._6_n-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Following this, the <i>Huainanzi</i> tells about how the sage-rulers Nüwa and <a href="/wiki/Fuxi" title="Fuxi">Fuxi</a> set order over the realm by following <a href="/wiki/Tao" title="Tao">the Way</a> (<span title="Chinese-language text"><span lang="zh">道</span></span>) and <a href="/wiki/De_(Chinese)" title="De (Chinese)">its potency</a> (<span title="Chinese-language text"><span lang="zh">德</span></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMajor_&amp;_al.2010ch._6_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMajor_&amp;_al.2010ch._6-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_of_Mountains_and_Seas" title="Classic of Mountains and Seas">Classic of Mountains and Seas</a></i>, dated between the <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">Warring States period</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a>, describes Nüwa's intestines as being scattered into ten spirits.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (October 2014)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Liezi" title="Liezi">Liezi</a></i> (c. 475 – 221 BC), Chapter 5 "Questions of Tang" (<span lang="zh-Hant">卷第五 湯問篇</span>), author <a href="/wiki/Lie_Yukou" title="Lie Yukou">Lie Yukou</a> describes Nüwa repairing the original imperfect heaven using five-colored <a href="/wiki/Stones" class="mw-redirect" title="Stones">stones</a>, and cutting the legs off a <a href="/wiki/Ao_(turtle)" title="Ao (turtle)">tortoise</a> to use as struts to hold up the sky.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Chuci" class="mw-redirect" title="Chuci">Songs of Chu</a></i> (c. 340 – 278 BC), Chapter 3 "<a href="/wiki/Heavenly_Questions" title="Heavenly Questions">Asking Heaven</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hans">问天</span>), author <a href="/wiki/Qu_Yuan" title="Qu Yuan">Qu Yuan</a> writes that Nüwa molded figures from the yellow earth, giving them life and the ability to bear children. After demons fought and broke the pillars of the heavens, Nüwa worked unceasingly to repair the damage, melting down the five-coloured stones to mend the heavens.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Shuowen_Jiezi" title="Shuowen Jiezi">Shuowen Jiezi</a></i> (c. 58 – 147 AD), China's earliest dictionary, under the entry for Nüwa author <a href="/wiki/Xu_Shen" title="Xu Shen">Xu Shen</a> describes her as being both the sister and the wife of <a href="/wiki/Fuxi" title="Fuxi">Fuxi</a>. Nüwa and Fuxi were pictured as having snake-like tails interlocked in an <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Han_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Han dynasty">Eastern Han dynasty</a> mural in the Wuliang Temple in Jiaxiang county, Shandong province.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Duyizhi" title="Duyizhi">Duyi Zhi</a></i> (<span lang="zh-Hant">獨異志</span>; c. 846 – 874 AD), Volume 3, author Li Rong gives this description.
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1244412712"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Long ago, when the world first began, there were two people, Nü Kua and her older brother. They lived on <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Mountain_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kunlun Mountain (mythology)">Mount K'un-lun</a>. And there were not yet any ordinary people in the world. They talked about becoming husband and wife, but they felt ashamed. So the brother at once went with his sister up <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Mountain_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kunlun Mountain (mythology)">Mount K'un-lun</a> and made this prayer: "Oh Heaven, if Thou wouldst send us two forth as man and wife, then make all the misty vapor gather. If not, then make all the misty vapor disperse." At this, the misty vapor immediately gathered. When the sister became intimate with her brother, they plaited some grass to make a fan to screen their faces. Even today, when a man takes a wife, they hold a fan, which is a symbol of what happened long ago.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>There are stories that have her as the "consort" of Fuxi rather than his sister.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuchuan_Ziji&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yuchuan Ziji (page does not exist)">Yuchuan Ziji</a></i> (<span lang="zh-Hans">玉川子集</span> c. 618 – 907 AD), Chapter 3 (<span lang="zh-Hans">"與馬異結交詩" 也稱 "女媧本是伏羲婦"</span>), author <a href="/wiki/Lu_Tong" title="Lu Tong">Lu Tong</a> describes Nüwa as the wife of Fuxi.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>
In <i><a href="/wiki/Siku_Quanshu" title="Siku Quanshu">Siku Quanshu</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Sima_Zhen" title="Sima Zhen">Sima Zhen</a> (679–732) provides commentary on the prologue chapter to <a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Shiji" title="Shiji">Shiji</a></i>, "Supplemental to the Historic Record: History of the Three August Ones", wherein it is found that the <a href="/wiki/Three_August_Ones_and_Five_Emperors" class="mw-redirect" title="Three August Ones and Five Emperors">Three August Ones</a> are Nüwa, Fuxi, and <a href="/wiki/Shennong" title="Shennong">Shennong</a>; Fuxi and Nüwa have the same last name, Feng (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">風</span>; Hmong: Faj).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>c<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright">

<p>In the collection <i><a href="/wiki/Songsi_Dashu" class="mw-redirect" title="Songsi Dashu">Four Great Books of Song</a></i> (c. 960 – 1279 AD), compiled by <a href="/wiki/Li_Fang_(Song_dynasty)" title="Li Fang (Song dynasty)">Li Fang</a> and others, Volume 78 of the book <i><a href="/wiki/Taiping_Yulan" title="Taiping Yulan">Imperial Readings of the Taiping Era</a></i> contains a chapter "Customs by Yingshao of the Han Dynasty" in which it is stated that there were no men when the sky and the earth were separated. Thus Nüwa used yellow clay to make people. But the clay was not strong enough so she put ropes into the <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> to make the bodies erect. It is also said that she prayed to gods to let her be the goddess of marital affairs. Variations of this story exist.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In Ming dynasty myths about the transition from the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a>, Nüwa made evil decisions that ultimately benefited <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, such as sending a <a href="/wiki/Fox_spirit" title="Fox spirit">fox spirit</a> to encourage the debauchery of <a href="/wiki/King_Zhou_of_Shang" title="King Zhou of Shang">King Zhou</a>, which led to him being deposed.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Other tales have her and Fuxi as exclusively the "great gentle protectors of humanity" unwilling to use subterfuge.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Nüwa and Fuxi were also thought to be gods of silk.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Iconography_of_Fuxi_and_Nüwa"><span id="Iconography_of_Fuxi_and_N.C3.BCwa"></span>Iconography of Fuxi and Nüwa</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%BCwa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Iconography of Fuxi and Nüwa"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:NuwaFuxi1.JPG" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/NuwaFuxi1.JPG/240px-NuwaFuxi1.JPG" decoding="async" width="240" height="232" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a9/NuwaFuxi1.JPG 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="290" /></a><figcaption>Nüwa and <a href="/wiki/Fuxi" title="Fuxi">Fuxi</a> on the murals (rubbing depicted) of the <a href="/wiki/Wu_Liang_shrines" class="mw-redirect" title="Wu Liang shrines">Wu Liang shrines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> (206 BC – 220 AD)</figcaption></figure><p>The iconography of Fuxi and Nüwa vary in physical appearance depending on the time period and also shows regional differences.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In Chinese tomb murals and iconography, Fuxi and Nüwa generally have snake-like bodies and human face or head.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Nüwa is often depicted holding a <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a> or multiple compasses, which were a traditional Chinese symbol of a dome-like sky.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> She was also thought to be an embodiment of the stars and the sky<sup id="cite_ref-:4_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or a star god.
</p><p>Fuxi and Nüwa can be depicted as individual figures arranged as a symmetrical pair or they can be depicted in double figures with intertwined snake-like bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Their snake-like tails can also be depicted stretching out towards each other.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This is similar to the representation of <a href="/wiki/Rahu" title="Rahu">Rahu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ketu_(mythology)" title="Ketu (mythology)">Ketu</a> in Indian astrology. 
</p><p>Fuxi and Nüwa can also appear individually on separate tomb bricks.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They generally hold or embrace the sun or moon discs containing the images of a <a href="/wiki/Three-legged_crow" title="Three-legged crow">bird</a> (or a three-legged crow) or a toad (sometimes a hare) which are the sun and moon symbolism respectively, and/or each holding a <a href="/wiki/Try_square" title="Try square">try square</a> or a pair of <a href="/wiki/Compass_(drawing_tool)" title="Compass (drawing tool)">compasses</a>, or holding a longevity mushroom (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">靈芝</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn">lingzhi</span></i>) plant.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Fuxi and Nüwa holding the sun and the moon appears as early as the late Western Han dynasty.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Other physical appearance variation, such as lower snake-like body shape (e.g. thick vs thin tails), depictions of legs (i.e. legs found along the snake-like body) and wings (e.g. wings with feathers which protrude from their backs as found in late Western Han Xinan (新安) Tomb or smaller quills found on their shoulders), and in hats and hairstyles, also exist.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In the Luoyang regions murals dating to the late <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Western Han dynasty</a>, Fuxi and Nüwa are generally depicted as individual figures, each one found at each side of the central ridge of tomb chambers as found in the Bu Qianqiu Tomb.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They can also be found without intertwining tails from the stone murals of the same period.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Since the middle of the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Eastern Han dynasty</a>, their tails started to intertwine.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>
In the Gansu murals dating to the <a href="/wiki/Cao_Wei" title="Cao Wei">Wei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jin_dynasty_(266%E2%80%93420)" title="Jin dynasty (266–420)">Western Jin</a> period, one of the most typical features of Fuxi is the "mountain-hat" (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">山字形</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn">Shanzixing</span></i>) which looks like a three-peaked cap while Nüwa is depicted wearing various hairstyles characteristic of Han women.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Both deities dressed in wide-sleeved clothing, which reflects typical <a href="/wiki/Hanfu" title="Hanfu">Han clothing style</a> also commonly depicted in Han dynasty art.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p><ul class="gallery mw-gallery-nolines">

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Legends">Legends</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%BCwa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legends"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Appearance_in_Fengshen_Yanyi">Appearance in <i>Fengshen Yanyi</i></h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%BCwa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Appearance in Fengshen Yanyi"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Anonymous-Fuxi_and_N%C3%BCwa.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Anonymous-Fuxi_and_N%C3%BCwa.jpg/220px-Anonymous-Fuxi_and_N%C3%BCwa.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="460" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Anonymous-Fuxi_and_N%C3%BCwa.jpg/330px-Anonymous-Fuxi_and_N%C3%BCwa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Anonymous-Fuxi_and_N%C3%BCwa.jpg/440px-Anonymous-Fuxi_and_N%C3%BCwa.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1013" data-file-height="2117" /></a><figcaption><div class="center" style="width:auto; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;">An ancient painting of Nüwa and Fuxi unearthed at the <a href="/wiki/Astana_Graves" class="mw-redirect" title="Astana Graves">Astana Graves</a>.</div></figcaption></figure>
<p>Nüwa is featured within the famed <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Investiture_of_the_Gods" title="Investiture of the Gods">Fengshen Bang</a></i>. As featured within this novel, Nüwa is revered since <a href="/wiki/Xia_dynasty" title="Xia dynasty">Xia dynasty</a> for creating the five-colored stones to mend the heavens, which tilted after <a href="/wiki/Gonggong" title="Gonggong">Gonggong</a> toppled one of the heavenly pillars, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Buzhou" title="Mount Buzhou">Mount Buzhou</a>. <a href="/wiki/Shang_Rong" title="Shang Rong">Shang Rong</a> asked <a href="/wiki/King_Zhou_of_Shang" title="King Zhou of Shang">King Zhou of Shang</a> to pay her a visit as a sign of deep respect. Upon seeing her statue, Zhou was completely overcome with lust at the sight of the beautiful ancient goddess Nüwa. He wrote an erotic poem on a neighboring wall and took his leave. When Nüwa later returned to her temple after visiting the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Emperor" title="Yellow Emperor">Yellow Emperor</a>, she saw the foulness of Zhou's words. In her anger, she swore that the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a> would end in payment for his offense. In her rage, Nüwa personally ascended to the palace in an attempt to kill the king, but was suddenly struck back by two large beams of red light.
</p><p>After Nüwa realized that <a href="/wiki/King_Zhou_of_Shang" title="King Zhou of Shang">King Zhou</a> was already destined to rule the kingdom for twenty-six more years, Nüwa summoned her three subordinates—the Thousand-Year Vixen (later becoming <a href="/wiki/Daji" title="Daji">Daji</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Pipa_Jing" title="Pipa Jing">Jade Pipa</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Jiutou_Zhiji_Jing" title="Jiutou Zhiji Jing">Nine-Headed Pheasant</a>. With these words, Nüwa brought destined chaos to the Shang dynasty, "The luck <a href="/wiki/Cheng_Tang" class="mw-redirect" title="Cheng Tang">Cheng Tang</a> won six hundred years ago is dimming. I speak to you of a new <a href="/wiki/Mandate_of_heaven" class="mw-redirect" title="Mandate of heaven">mandate of heaven</a> which sets the destiny for all. You three are to enter King Zhou's palace, where you are to bewitch him. Whatever you do, do not harm anyone else. If you do my bidding, and do it well, you will be permitted to reincarnate as human beings."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> With these words, Nüwa was never heard of again, but was still a major indirect factor towards the Shang dynasty's fall.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Creation_of_humanity">Creation of humanity</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%BCwa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Creation of humanity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pangu" title="Pangu">Pangu</a> was said to be the creation god in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mythology" title="Chinese mythology">Chinese mythology</a>. He was a giant sleeping within an egg of chaos. As he awoke, he stood up and divided the sky and the earth. Pangu then died after standing up, and his body turned into rivers, mountains, plants, animals, and everything else in the world, among which is a powerful being known as <a href="/wiki/Huaxu" title="Huaxu">Huaxu</a> (華胥). Huaxu gave birth to a twin brother and sister, Fuxi and Nüwa. Fuxi and Nüwa are said to be creatures that have faces of human and bodies of snakes.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Nüwa created humanity due to her loneliness, which grew more intense over time. She molded yellow earth or, in other versions, yellow clay into the shape of people. These individuals later became the wealthy nobles of society, because they had been created by Nüwa's own hands. However, the majority of humanity was created when Nüwa dragged string across mud to mass-produce them, which she did because creating every person by hand was too time- and energy-consuming. This creation story gives an <a href="/wiki/Etiology" title="Etiology">aetiological</a> explanation for the <a href="/wiki/Social_hierarchy" class="mw-redirect" title="Social hierarchy">social hierarchy</a> in ancient China. The nobility believed that they were more important than the mass-produced majority of humanity, because Nüwa took time to create them, and they had been directly touched by her hand.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In another version of the creation of humanity, Nüwa and <a href="/wiki/Fuxi" title="Fuxi">Fuxi</a> were survivors of a great flood. By the command of the God of the heaven, they were married and Nüwa had a child which was a ball of meat. This ball of meat was cut into small pieces, and the pieces were scattered across the world, which then became humans.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Nüwa was born three months after her brother, <a href="/wiki/Fuxi" title="Fuxi">Fuxi</a>, whom she later took as her husband; this marriage is the reason why Nüwa is credited with inventing the idea of marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Before the two of them got married, they lived on mount <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Mountains" title="Kunlun Mountains">K'un-lun</a>. A prayer was made after the two became guilty of falling for each other. The prayer is as follows,
</p><p>"Oh Heaven, if Thou wouldst send us forth as man and wife, then make all the misty vapor gather. If not, then make all the misty vapor disperse."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Misty vapor then gathered after the prayer signifying the two could marry. When intimate, the two made a fan out of grass to screen their faces which is why during modern day marriages, the couple hold a fan together. By connecting, the two were representative of <a href="/wiki/Yin_and_yang" title="Yin and yang">Yin and Yang</a>; <a href="/wiki/Fuxi" title="Fuxi">Fuxi</a> being connected to <a href="/wiki/Yin_and_yang" title="Yin and yang">Yang</a> and masculinity along Nüwa being connected to <a href="/wiki/Yin_and_yang" title="Yin and yang">Yin</a> and femininity. This is further defined with Fuxi receiving a <a href="/wiki/Steel_square" title="Steel square">carpenter's square</a> which symbolizes his identification with the physical world because a carpenter's square is associated with straight lines and squares leading to a more straightforward mindset. Meanwhile, Nüwa was given a <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a> to symbolize her identification with the heavens because a compass is associated with curves and circles leading to a more abstract mindset. With the two being married, it symbolized the union between heaven and Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Other versions have Nüwa invent the compass rather than receive it as a gift.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In addition, the system of male and female sex, the yang-yin philosophy, is expressed here in a complex way: first as Fuxi and Nüwa, then as a compass (masculine) and a square (feminine), and thirdly, as Nüwa (woman) with a compass (man) and Fuxi (man) with a square (woman).<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Nüwa_Mends_the_Heavens"><span id="N.C3.BCwa_Mends_the_Heavens"></span>Nüwa Mends the Heavens</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%BCwa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nüwa Mends the Heavens"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Ping_Sien_Si_-_065_Nu_Wa_Niang_Niang_(16140351273).jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Ping_Sien_Si_-_065_Nu_Wa_Niang_Niang_%2816140351273%29.jpg/220px-Ping_Sien_Si_-_065_Nu_Wa_Niang_Niang_%2816140351273%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="398" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Ping_Sien_Si_-_065_Nu_Wa_Niang_Niang_%2816140351273%29.jpg/330px-Ping_Sien_Si_-_065_Nu_Wa_Niang_Niang_%2816140351273%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Ping_Sien_Si_-_065_Nu_Wa_Niang_Niang_%2816140351273%29.jpg/440px-Ping_Sien_Si_-_065_Nu_Wa_Niang_Niang_%2816140351273%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2167" data-file-height="3916" /></a><figcaption>Modern relief of Nüwa at the Ping Sien Si Temple in Perak, Malaysia</figcaption></figure>
<p><b>Nüwa Mends the Heavens</b> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">女娲補天</span>; <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hans">女娲补天</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn">Nǚwā bǔtiān</span></i>) is a well-known theme in <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Culture of China">Chinese culture</a>. The courage and wisdom of Nüwa inspired the ancient Chinese to control nature's elements and has become a favorite subject of Chinese <a href="/wiki/Poets" class="mw-redirect" title="Poets">poets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Painters" class="mw-redirect" title="Painters">painters</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sculptors" class="mw-redirect" title="Sculptors">sculptors</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> along with so many poetry and arts like novels, films, paintings, and sculptures; e.g. the sculptures that decorate <a href="/wiki/Nanshan_District,_Shenzhen" class="mw-redirect" title="Nanshan District, Shenzhen">Nanshan</a><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Yaan" class="mw-redirect" title="Yaan">Ya'an</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Huainanzi" title="Huainanzi">Huainanzi</a> tells an ancient story about how the four pillars that support the sky crumbled inexplicably. Other sources have tried to explain the cause, i.e. the battle between <a href="/wiki/Gonggong" title="Gonggong">Gong Gong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhuanxu" title="Zhuanxu">Zhuanxu</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zhurong" title="Zhurong">Zhurong</a>. Unable to accept his defeat, Gong Gong deliberately banged his head onto <a href="/wiki/Mount_Buzhou" title="Mount Buzhou">Mount Buzhou</a> (不周山) which was one of the four pillars. Half of the sky fell which created a gaping hole and the Earth itself was cracked; the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Axis_mundi" title="Axis mundi">axis mundi</a> was tilted into the southeast while the sky rose into the northwest. This is said to be the reason why the western region of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> is higher than the eastern and that most of its rivers flow towards the southeast. This same explanation is applied to the Sun, Moon, and stars which moved into the northwest. A wildfire burnt the forests and led the wild animals to run amok and attack the innocent peoples, while the water which was coming out from the earth's crack didn't seem to be slowing down.<sup id="cite_ref-yang_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yang-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Nüwa pitied the humans she had made and attempted to repair the sky. She gathered <a href="/wiki/Color_in_Chinese_culture" title="Color in Chinese culture">five colored-stones (red, yellow, blue, black, and white)</a> from the riverbed, melted them and used them to patch up the sky: since then the sky (clouds) have been colorful. She then killed a giant turtle (or tortoise), some version named the tortoise as <a href="/wiki/Ao_(turtle)" title="Ao (turtle)">Ao</a>, cut off the four legs of the creature to use as new pillars to support the sky. But Nüwa didn't do it perfectly because the unequal length of the legs made the sky tilt. After the job was done, Nüwa drove away the wild animals, extinguished the fire, and controlled the flood with a huge amount of ashes from the burning reeds and the world became as peaceful as it was before.<sup id="cite_ref-yang_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yang-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Empress_Nüwa"><span id="Empress_N.C3.BCwa"></span>Empress Nüwa</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%BCwa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Empress Nüwa"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Many Chinese know well their <a href="/wiki/Three_Sovereigns_and_Five_Emperors" title="Three Sovereigns and Five Emperors">Three Sovereigns and Five Emperors</a>, i.e. the early leaders of humanity as well as <a href="/wiki/Culture_heroes" class="mw-redirect" title="Culture heroes">culture heroes</a> according to <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mythology" title="Chinese mythology">the Northern Chinese belief</a>. But the lists vary and depend on the sources used.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> One version includes Nüwa as one of the Three Sovereigns, who reigned after <a href="/wiki/Fuxi" title="Fuxi">Fuxi</a> and before <a href="/wiki/Shennong" title="Shennong">Shennong</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The myth of the Three Sovereigns sees the three as demigod figures, and the myth is used to stress the importance of an imperial reign. The variation between sources stems from China being generally divided before the Qin and Han dynasties, and the version with Fuxi, Shennong, and Nüwa was used to emphasize rule and structure.<sup id="cite_ref-ty_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ty-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In her matriarchal reign, she battled against a neighboring tribal chief, defeated him, and took him to the peak of a mountain. Defeated by a woman, the chief felt ashamed to be alive and banged his head on the heavenly bamboo to kill himself and for revenge. His act tore a hole in the sky and made a flood hit the whole world. The flood killed all people except Nüwa and her army which was protected by her divinity. After that, Nüwa patched the sky with five colored-stones until the flood receded.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=N%C3%BCwa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Popular culture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> fantasy novel <i><a href="/wiki/Investiture_of_the_Gods" title="Investiture of the Gods">Investiture of the Gods</a></i> (1567) has Nüwa being an instigator of the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a>'s collapse, as she sent the <a href="/wiki/Hulijing" class="mw-redirect" title="Hulijing">fox demon</a> <a href="/wiki/Daji" title="Daji">Daji</a> to corrupt <a href="/wiki/King_Zhou_of_Shang" title="King Zhou of Shang">King Zhou</a> for the latter verbally <a href="/wiki/Desecrating" class="mw-redirect" title="Desecrating">desecrating</a> her statue at a temple.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Dream_of_the_Red_Chamber" title="Dream of the Red Chamber">Dream of the Red Chamber</a></i> (1754) narrates how Nüwa gathered 36,501 stones to patch the sky but left one unused. The unused stone plays an important role in the novel's storyline.</li>
<li>A goddess Nüwa statue named <i>Sky Patching</i> by <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Xikun" title="Yuan Xikun">Yuan Xikun</a> was exhibited at <a href="/wiki/Times_Square" title="Times Square">Times Square</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, on 19 April 2012 to celebrate <a href="/wiki/Earth_Day" title="Earth Day">Earth Day</a> (2012), symbolized the importance of protecting the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Previously, this 3.9 meter tall statue was exhibited on <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> and now is placed on <a href="/wiki/Vienna_International_Centre" title="Vienna International Centre">Vienna International Centre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> since 21 November 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The story of Nüwa patching the sky was being retold by Carol Chen in her book <i>Goddess Nuwa Patches Up the Sky</i> (2014) which was illustrated by Meng Xianlong.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Shin_Megami_Tensei_5" class="mw-redirect" title="Shin Megami Tensei 5">Shin Megami Tensei 5</a>, Nuwa (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Ayana_Taketatsu" title="Ayana Taketatsu">Ayana Taketatsu</a>) is the partner to Shohei Yakumo (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Tomokazu_Sugita" title="Tomokazu Sugita">Tomokazu Sugita</a>) as two of the main characters who aid the protagonist.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Gremlins_(TV_series)" title="Gremlins (TV series)"><i>Gremlins</i></a> animated series, Nuwa (voiced by <a href="/wiki/Sandra_Oh" title="Sandra Oh">Sandra Oh</a>) is portrayed as the creator of the Mogwai species that Gizmo originated from and fell into a depression when the humans could not properly coexist with them.</li></ul>
