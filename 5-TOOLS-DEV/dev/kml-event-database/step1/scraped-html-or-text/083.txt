<p><b>Ogyges</b>, also spelled <b>Ogygus</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">Ὠγύγης or Ὤγυγος</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Ancient_Greek" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanization of Ancient Greek">romanized</a>:&#160;</small><span title="Ancient Greek-language romanization"><i lang="grc-Latn">Ogygos</i></span>), is a primeval <a href="/wiki/Mythology" class="mw-redirect" title="Mythology">mythological</a> ruler in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a>, generally of <a href="/wiki/Boeotia" title="Boeotia">Boeotia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> but an alternative tradition makes him the first king of <a href="/wiki/Attica" title="Attica">Attica</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Etymology">Etymology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ogyges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Though the original etymology and meaning are "uncertain",<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> the name <i>Ogyges</i> may be related to the Greek <a href="/wiki/Okeanos" class="mw-redirect" title="Okeanos">Okeanos</a> (Ὠκεανός), the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Titan (mythology)">Titan</a> who personified the great world <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Greek word <i>Ogygios</i> (Ὠγύγιος), meaning <i>Ogygian</i>, came to mean "primeval, primal," or "from earliest ages" and also "gigantic".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Family">Family</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ogyges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Stories of Ogyges's descent differs widely. Besides Ogyges being one of the aborigines of Boeotia, there are tales that regard him as the son of <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a> (by <a href="/wiki/Alistra_(mythology)" title="Alistra (mythology)">Alistra</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an AI-generated source. (November 2024)">AI-generated source?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Boeotus_(son_of_Poseidon)" title="Boeotus (son of Poseidon)">Boeotus</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or even <a href="/wiki/Cadmus" title="Cadmus">Cadmus</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. <a href="/wiki/Theophilus_of_Antioch" title="Theophilus of Antioch">Theophilus</a>, in the 2nd century (<i>Apologia ad Autolycum</i>), says he was one of the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Titan (mythology)">Titans</a>.
</p><p>Ogyges was the husband of <a href="/wiki/Thebe_(Greek_myth)" title="Thebe (Greek myth)">Thebe</a>, from whom the land of Thebes in Greece is said to derive its name.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an AI-generated source. (November 2024)">AI-generated source?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> His children are listed variously as two sons: <a href="/wiki/Eleusis_(mythology)" title="Eleusis (mythology)">Eleusinus</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> (for whom the city Eleusis was named) and Cadmus (noted above as his father in other traditions); and three daughters: <a href="/wiki/Aulis_(mythology)" title="Aulis (mythology)">Aulis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alalcomenia" title="Alalcomenia">Alalcomenia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thelxino%C3%AB" title="Thelxinoë">Thelxinia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stoa.org_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stoa.org-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Mythology">Mythology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ogyges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mythology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Reign">Reign</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ogyges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reign"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Ogyges is also known as king of the <a href="/wiki/Ectenes" title="Ectenes">Ectenes</a>, who according to <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a> were the first inhabitants of Boeotia, where the city of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Thebes_(Boeotia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Thebes (Boeotia)">Thebes</a> would later be founded.<sup id="cite_ref-Seyffert_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seyffert-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> As such, he became the first ruler of Thebes, which was, in that early time, named <i>Ogygia</i> (Ὠγυγία) after him. Subsequently, poets referred to the Thebans as <i>Ogygidae</i> (Ὠγυγίδαι).<sup id="cite_ref-Blakeney_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blakeney-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a>, writing from his travels in Boeotia in the 2nd century CE, said: "The first to occupy the land of Thebes are said to have been the Ectenes, whose king was Ogygus, an aboriginal. From his name is derived Ogygian, which is an epithet of Thebes used by most of the poets."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In yet another version of the story, the Boeotian tradition is combined with that of another part of Greece: Ogyges was king of the Ectenes, who were the first people to occupy Boeotia, but he and his people later settled the area then known as <a href="/wiki/Mount_Athos" title="Mount Athos">Acte</a> (<i>Akte</i>). The land was subsequently called Ogygia in his honor but later known as Mount Athos. <a href="/wiki/Sextus_Julius_Africanus" title="Sextus Julius Africanus">Sextus Julius Africanus</a>, writing after 221 CE, adds that Ogyges founded <a href="/wiki/Eleusis" class="mw-redirect" title="Eleusis">Eleusis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chronography_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chronography-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In one account, his predecessor was called <a href="/wiki/Calydnus" title="Calydnus">Kalydnos</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Uranus_(mythology)" title="Uranus (mythology)">Ouranos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>According to Africanus, Ogygus lived at the time of the <a href="/wiki/The_Exodus" title="The Exodus">Exodus</a> of the House of Israel from Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-Chronography_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chronography-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Ogyges is possibly the namesake for the <a href="/wiki/Phantom_island" title="Phantom island">phantom island</a> <a href="/wiki/Ogygia" title="Ogygia">Ogygia</a>, mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a>. A long-standing tradition begun by <a href="/wiki/Euhemerus" title="Euhemerus">Euhemerus</a> in the late 4th century BC and supported by <a href="/wiki/Callimachus" title="Callimachus">Callimachus</a>, endorsed by modern <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Maltese</a> tradition, identifies Ogygia with the island of <a href="/wiki/Gozo" title="Gozo">Gozo</a>, the second largest island in the <a href="/wiki/Maltese_archipelago" class="mw-redirect" title="Maltese archipelago">Maltese archipelago</a>. Another possibility for the island is the Niobid named <a href="/wiki/Niobids" title="Niobids">Ogygia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2024)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The historian <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Josephus</a> mentions Ogyges as the name of the oak by which the <a href="/wiki/Hebrews" title="Hebrews">Hebrew</a> patriarch <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abram</a> dwelt while he lived near <a href="/wiki/Hebron" title="Hebron">Hebron</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Og" title="Og">Og</a>, also called "Ogias the Giant", who was king of <a href="/wiki/Bashan" title="Bashan">Bashan</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>; was described as a giant in Deut 3:11, viewed by the <a href="/wiki/Hebrews" title="Hebrews">Hebrews</a> as having aided <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a> in building the <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Ark" title="Noah&#39;s Ark">Ark</a>, thus Noah allowed him to stay on the deck of the Ark.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="The_deluge_of_Ogyges">The deluge of Ogyges</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ogyges&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The deluge of Ogyges"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Boeotia_ancient-en.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Boeotia_ancient-en.svg/220px-Boeotia_ancient-en.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Boeotia_ancient-en.svg/330px-Boeotia_ancient-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Boeotia_ancient-en.svg/440px-Boeotia_ancient-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="646" data-file-height="442" /></a><figcaption>Map of ancient Boeotia. The area around the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Copais" title="Lake Copais">Lake Copais</a> down to <a href="/wiki/Attica" title="Attica">Attica</a> is related with the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_flood_myths#Ogyges" title="Ancient Greek flood myths">Ogygian deluge</a></figcaption></figure>
<p>The first worldwide flood in Greek mythology, the <a href="/wiki/Ogygian_deluge" class="mw-redirect" title="Ogygian deluge">Ogygian deluge</a> occurred during his reign and derives its name from him, though some sources regard it as a local flood, such as an inundation of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Copais" title="Lake Copais">Lake Copais</a>, a large lake once in the center of Boeotia.<sup id="cite_ref-Seyffert_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seyffert-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Other sources see it as a flood associated with Attica.<sup id="cite_ref-Blakeney_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blakeney-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This latter view was accepted by Africanus, who says "that great and first flood occurred in Attica, when <a href="/wiki/Phoroneus" title="Phoroneus">Phoroneus</a> was king of <a href="/wiki/Argos,_Peloponnese" title="Argos, Peloponnese">Argos</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Acusilaus" title="Acusilaus">Acusilaus</a> relates."
</p><p>When this deluge has been considered global, a similarity is noticed with <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a>'s flood in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>. Various dates have been assigned to the event, including 2136 BCE (<a href="/wiki/Marcus_Terentius_Varro" title="Marcus Terentius Varro">Varro</a>), and 1793 BCE (Africanus).<sup id="cite_ref-Chronography_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chronography-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Ogyges survived the deluge but many people perished. After his death, the devastated Attica was without kings for 189 years, until the time of <a href="/wiki/Cecrops_I" title="Cecrops I">Cecrops</a> (Cecrops Diphyes).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Africanus says, "But after Ogyges, on account of the great destruction caused by the flood, what is now called Attica remained without a king one hundred and eighty-nine years until the time of Cecrops. For <a href="/wiki/Philochorus" title="Philochorus">Philochorus</a> asserts that that <a href="/wiki/Actaeus" title="Actaeus">Actaeon</a> who comes after Ogyges, and the fictitious names, never even existed."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>It seems the <a href="/wiki/Deluge_myth" class="mw-redirect" title="Deluge myth">deluge</a> of <a href="/wiki/Deucalion" title="Deucalion">Deucalion</a> of Greek-mythology is the Greek version of the older legend. <a href="/wiki/Deucalion" title="Deucalion">Deucalion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyrrha" title="Pyrrha">Pyrrha</a> were the only survivors after the great deluge. Their son <a href="/wiki/Hellen" title="Hellen">Hellen</a>, who became ruler of <a href="/wiki/Phthia" title="Phthia">Phthia</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Thessaly" title="Thessaly">Thessaly</a>, was the patriarch of the <a href="/wiki/Hellenes" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenes">Hellenes</a>.
</p>
