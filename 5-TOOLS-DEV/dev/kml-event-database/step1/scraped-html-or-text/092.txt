					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Greek mythological figure</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1236090951">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Deucalion_(mythology)" title="Deucalion (mythology)">Deucalion (mythology)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deucalion_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Deucalion (disambiguation)">Deucalion (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Deucalion.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Deucalion.jpg/220px-Deucalion.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="225" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Deucalion.jpg/330px-Deucalion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/41/Deucalion.jpg 2x" data-file-width="426" data-file-height="435" /></a><figcaption>Deucalion from <i><a href="/wiki/Promptuarium_Iconum_Insigniorum" title="Promptuarium Iconum Insigniorum">Promptuarium Iconum Insigniorum</a></i></figcaption></figure>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <b>Deucalion</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/dj/: &#39;d&#39; in &#39;dew&#39;">dj</span><span title="/uː/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">uː</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span><span title="/ən/: &#39;on&#39; in &#39;button&#39;">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%94%CE%B5%CF%85%CE%BA%CE%B1%CE%BB%CE%AF%CF%89%CE%BD#Ancient_Greek" class="extiw" title="wikt:Δευκαλίων">Δευκαλίων</a></span>) was the son of <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a>; ancient sources name his mother as <a href="/wiki/Clymene_(mythology)" title="Clymene (mythology)">Clymene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hesione_(Oceanid)" title="Hesione (Oceanid)">Hesione</a>, or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pronoia_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pronoia (mythology) (page does not exist)">Pronoia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pronoia_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pronoia-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He is closely connected with a <a href="/wiki/Flood_myth" title="Flood myth">flood myth</a> in Greek mythology.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Etymology">Etymology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deucalion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a>, Deucalion's name comes from <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">δεῦκος</span></span>, <i>deukos</i>, a variant of <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">γλεῦκος</span></span>, <i>gleucos</i>, i.e. "sweet new wine, must, sweetness"<sup id="cite_ref-LSJ_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LSJ-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LSJ1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LSJ1-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and from <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">ἁλιεύς</span></span>, <i>haliéus</i>, i.e. "sailor, seaman, fisher".<sup id="cite_ref-LSJ2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LSJ2-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> His wife <a href="/wiki/Pyrrha" title="Pyrrha">Pyrrha</a>'s name derives from the adjective <span title="Ancient Greek (to 1453)-language text"><span lang="grc">πυρρός</span></span>, -ά, -όν, <i>pyrrhós, -á, -ón</i>, i.e. "flame-colored, orange".<sup id="cite_ref-LSJ3_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LSJ3-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Family">Family</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deucalion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Of Deucalion's birth, the <i><a href="/wiki/Argonautica" title="Argonautica">Argonautica</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> (from the 3rd century BC) stated:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There [in <a href="/wiki/Achaea" title="Achaea">Achaea</a>, i.e. Greece] is a land encircled by lofty mountains, rich in sheep and in pasture, where <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Iapetus" title="Iapetus">Iapetus</a>, begat goodly Deucalion, who first founded cities and reared temples to the immortal gods, and first ruled over men. This land the neighbours who dwell around call <i>Haemonia</i> [i.e. <a href="/wiki/Thessaly" title="Thessaly">Thessaly</a>].</p></blockquote>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_(Pseudo-Apollodorus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bibliotheca (Pseudo-Apollodorus)">Bibliotheca</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Deucalion and Pyrrha had at least two children, <a href="/wiki/Hellen" title="Hellen">Hellen</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Protogeneia" title="Protogeneia">Protogenea</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and possibly a third, <a href="/wiki/Amphictyon" title="Amphictyon">Amphictyon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Another account, adds a daughter <a href="/wiki/Melanthea" title="Melanthea">Melanthea</a> to the list of the couple’s progeny.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This daughter, also called  <a href="/wiki/Melantho" title="Melantho">Melantho</a>, became the mother of <a href="/wiki/Delphus" title="Delphus">Delphus</a> by <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">Poseidon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an AI-generated source. (November 2024)">AI-generated source?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Deucalion’s and Pyrrha’s children are apparently named in one of the oldest texts, <i><a href="/wiki/Catalogue_of_Women" title="Catalogue of Women">Catalogue of Women</a></i>, include daughters <a href="/wiki/Pandora_of_Thessaly" class="mw-redirect" title="Pandora of Thessaly">Pandora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thyia_of_Thessaly" class="mw-redirect" title="Thyia of Thessaly">Thyia</a>, and at least one son, Hellen.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Their descendants were said to have dwelt and ruled in Thessaly.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>One source mentioned three sons of Deucalion and his wife: <a href="/wiki/Orestheus" title="Orestheus">Orestheus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marathonius" title="Marathonius">Marathonios</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pronous_(mythology)" title="Pronous (mythology)">Pronous</a> (father of Hellen).<sup id="cite_ref-:2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Lastly, Deucalion sired a son, no mention of the mother, <a href="/wiki/Candybus" title="Candybus">Candybus</a> who gave his name to the town of <a href="/wiki/Kandyba" title="Kandyba">Candyba</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lycia" title="Lycia">Lycia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p>

<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Virgil_Solis_-_Deucalion_Pyrrha.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Virgil_Solis_-_Deucalion_Pyrrha.jpg/250px-Virgil_Solis_-_Deucalion_Pyrrha.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="190" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Virgil_Solis_-_Deucalion_Pyrrha.jpg/375px-Virgil_Solis_-_Deucalion_Pyrrha.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Virgil_Solis_-_Deucalion_Pyrrha.jpg/500px-Virgil_Solis_-_Deucalion_Pyrrha.jpg 2x" data-file-width="610" data-file-height="463" /></a><figcaption>Deucalion and <a href="/wiki/Pyrrha" title="Pyrrha">Pyrrha</a> from a 1562 version of Ovid's <i><a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a></i></figcaption></figure>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Deluge_accounts">Deluge accounts</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deucalion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Deluge accounts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The flood in the time of Deucalion was caused by the anger of <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>, ignited by the <a href="/wiki/Hubris" title="Hubris">hubris</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lycaon_(king_of_Arcadia)" title="Lycaon (king of Arcadia)">Lycaon</a> and his sons, descendants of <a href="/wiki/Pelasgus" title="Pelasgus">Pelasgus</a>. According to this story, King Lycaon of <a href="/wiki/Arcadia_(ancient_region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Arcadia (ancient region)">Arcadia</a> had sacrificed a boy to Zeus, who, appalled by this offering, decided to put an end to the <a href="/wiki/Ages_of_Man" title="Ages of Man">"Bronze" Age</a> by unleashing a deluge. During this catastrophic flood, the rivers ran in torrents and the sea flooded the coastal plain, engulfing the foothills with spray, and washing everything clean.
</p><p>Deucalion, with the aid of his father Prometheus, was saved from this deluge by building a chest.<sup id="cite_ref-When_the_Great_Abyss_Opened_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-When_the_Great_Abyss_Opened-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Like the biblical <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a> and the Mesopotamian counterpart <a href="/wiki/Utnapishtim" title="Utnapishtim">Utnapishtim</a>, he used this device to survive the <a href="/wiki/Flood_myth" title="Flood myth">great flood</a> with his wife, Pyrrha.
</p><p>The most complete accounts are given by <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>, in his <i><a href="/wiki/Metamorphoses" title="Metamorphoses">Metamorphoses</a></i> (late 1 BCE to early 1 CE), and by the mythographer <a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_(Pseudo-Apollodorus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bibliotheca (Pseudo-Apollodorus)">Apollodorus</a> (1st or 2nd century CE).<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Deucalion, who reigned over the region of <a href="/wiki/Phthia" title="Phthia">Phthia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> had been forewarned of the flood by his father Prometheus. Deucalion was to build a chest and provision it carefully (no animals are rescued in this version of the flood myth), so that when the waters receded after nine days, he and his wife <a href="/wiki/Pyrrha" title="Pyrrha">Pyrrha</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Epimetheus_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Epimetheus (mythology)">Epimetheus</a>, were the one surviving pair of humans. Their chest touched solid ground on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Parnassus" title="Mount Parnassus">Mount Parnassus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Mount_Etna" title="Mount Etna">Mount Etna</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Mount_Athos" title="Mount Athos">Mount Athos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chalkidiki" title="Chalkidiki">Chalkidiki</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Mount_Othrys" title="Mount Othrys">Mount Othrys</a> in Thessaly.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gaius_Julius_Hyginus" title="Gaius Julius Hyginus">Hyginus</a> mentioned the opinion of a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hegesianax&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hegesianax (page does not exist)">Hegesianax</a> that Deucalion is to be identified with <a href="/wiki/Aquarius_(astrology)" title="Aquarius (astrology)">Aquarius</a>, "because during his reign such quantities of water poured from the sky that the great Flood resulted."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Once the deluge was over and the couple had given thanks to Zeus, Deucalion (said in several of the sources to have been aged 82 at the time) consulted an <a href="/wiki/Oracle" title="Oracle">oracle</a> of <a href="/wiki/Themis" title="Themis">Themis</a> about how to repopulate the earth. He was told to "cover your head and throw the bones of your mother behind your shoulder". Deucalion and Pyrrha understood that "mother" was <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaia (mythology)">Gaia</a>, the mother of all living things, and the "bones" to be rocks. They threw the rocks behind their shoulders and the stones formed people. Pyrrha's became women; Deucalion's became men.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These people were later called the <a href="/wiki/Leleges" title="Leleges">Leleges</a> who populated <a href="/wiki/Locris" title="Locris">Locris</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This can be related to <a href="/wiki/Pindar" title="Pindar">Pindar's</a> account that recounted "Pyrrha and Deucalion came down from Parnassus and made their first home, and without the marriage-bed they founded a unified race of stone offspring, and the stones gave the people their name."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The 2nd-century AD writer <a href="/wiki/Lucian" title="Lucian">Lucian</a> gave an account of the Greek Deucalion in <i><a href="/wiki/De_Dea_Syria" class="mw-redirect" title="De Dea Syria">De Dea Syria</a></i> that seems to refer more to the Near Eastern flood legends: in his version, Deucalion (whom he also calls <i>Sisythus</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> took his children, their wives, and pairs of animals with him on the ark, and later built a great temple in <a href="/wiki/Manbij" title="Manbij">Manbij</a> (northern Syria), on the site of the chasm that received all the waters; he further describes how pilgrims brought vessels of sea water to this place twice a year, from as far as Arabia and Mesopotamia, to commemorate this event.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Variant_stories">Variant stories</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deucalion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Variant stories"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_of_Halicarnassus" title="Dionysius of Halicarnassus">Dionysius of Halicarnassus</a> stated Deucalion's parents to be Prometheus and Clymene, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">Oceanus</a>, and mentioned nothing about a flood but instead named him as commander of those from Parnassus who drove the "sixth generation" of <a href="/wiki/Pelasgians" title="Pelasgians">Pelasgians</a> from Thessaly.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>One of the earliest Greek historians, <a href="/wiki/Hecataeus_of_Miletus" title="Hecataeus of Miletus">Hecataeus of Miletus</a>, was said to have written a book about Deucalion, but it no longer survived. The only extant fragment of his to mention Deucalion does not mention the flood either, but named him as the father of Orestheus, king of <a href="/wiki/Aetolia" title="Aetolia">Aetolia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The much later geographer <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a>, following on this tradition, named Deucalion as a king of <a href="/wiki/Ozolian_Locris" title="Ozolian Locris">Ozolian Locris</a> and father of Orestheus.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a> mentioned a legend that Deucalion and Pyrrha had settled in <a href="/wiki/Dodona" title="Dodona">Dodona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epirus" title="Epirus">Epirus</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> asserted that they lived at <a href="/wiki/Opus,_Greece" title="Opus, Greece">Cynus</a>, and that her grave was still to be found there, while his may be seen at <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This can be related to an account that after the deluge, Deucalion, founder and king of <a href="/wiki/Lycoreia" title="Lycoreia">Lycoreia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mount_Parnassus" title="Mount Parnassus">Mt. Parnassus</a><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> was said to have fled from his kingdom to Athens with his sons Hellen and Amphictyon during the reign of King <a href="/wiki/Cranaus" title="Cranaus">Cranaus</a>. Shortly thereafter, Deucalion died there and was said to have been buried near Athens.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During his stay in there, he was credited with having built the ancient sanctuary of Olympian Zeus.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Additionally, Strabo mentioned a pair of <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Islands" title="Aegean Islands">Aegean islands</a> named after the couple.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Interpretation">Interpretation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deucalion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Interpretation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Mosaic_accretions">Mosaic accretions</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deucalion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mosaic accretions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The 19th-century classicist <a href="/wiki/John_Lempri%C3%A8re" title="John Lemprière">John Lemprière</a>, in <i><a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_Classica" title="Bibliotheca Classica">Bibliotheca Classica</a></i>, argued that as the story had been re-told in later versions, it accumulated details from the stories of Noah: "Thus Apollodorus gives Deucalion a great chest as a means of safety; Plutarch speaks of the pigeons by which he sought to find out whether the waters had receded; and Lucian of the animals of every kind which he had taken with him. &amp;c."<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, the Epic of Gilgamesh contains each of the three elements identified by Lemprière: a means of safety (in the form of instructions to build a boat), sending forth birds to test whether the waters had receded, and stowing animals of every kind on the boat.  These facts were unknown to Lemprière because the Assyrian cuneiform tablets containing the Gilgamesh Epic were not discovered until in the 1850s.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This was 20 years after Lemprière had published his "Bibliotheca Classica". The Gilgamesh epic is widely considered to be at least as old as Genesis, if not older.<sup id="cite_ref-George2003_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-George2003-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  Given the prevalence of religious syncretism in the ancient Greek world, these three elements may already have been known to some Greek-speaking peoples in popular oral variations of the flood myth, long before they were recorded in writing. The most immediate source of these three particular elements in the later Greek versions is unclear.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Dating_by_early_scholars">Dating by early scholars</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deucalion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dating by early scholars"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>For some time during the Middle Ages, many European Christian scholars continued to accept Greek mythical history at face value, thus asserting that Deucalion's flood was a regional flood, that occurred a few centuries later than the global one survived by Noah's family. On the basis of the archaeological <i><a href="/wiki/Stele" title="Stele">stele</a></i> known as the <a href="/wiki/Parian_Chronicle" title="Parian Chronicle">Parian Chronicle</a>, Deucalion's Flood was usually fixed as occurring some time around 1528 BC. Deucalion's flood may be dated in the <a href="/wiki/Chronicon_(Jerome)" title="Chronicon (Jerome)">chronology</a> of Saint <a href="/wiki/Jerome" title="Jerome">Jerome</a> to <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1460 BC. According to <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Augustine of Hippo</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/City_of_God_(book)" class="mw-redirect" title="City of God (book)">City of God</a></i> XVIII,8,10,&amp;11), Deucalion and his father Prometheus were contemporaries of Moses. According to <a href="/wiki/Clement_of_Alexandria" title="Clement of Alexandria">Clement of Alexandria</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Stromata" title="Stromata">Stromata</a>, "in the time of <a href="/wiki/Crotopus" title="Crotopus">Crotopus</a> occurred the burning of <a href="/wiki/Phaethon" title="Phaethon">Phaethon</a>, and the deluges of Deucalion."<sup id="cite_ref-Book1_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Book1-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
